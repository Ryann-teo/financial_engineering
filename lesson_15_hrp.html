0. Reading the controls

Linkage method

Single (nearest neighbour), complete (farthest), average, Ward (variance-minimising). Each produces a different dendrogram, hence different HRP weights.

Block strength

How clustered the universe is. With strong block structure, HRP exploits the hierarchy. With uniform correlation, HRP looks like inverse-vol.

Noise level

Add Gaussian noise to the correlation matrix. Watch how HRP weights stay stable while min-var weights jump.

1. Why no inverse?

Lesson 9 showed that $\Sigma^{-1}$ amplifies estimation error. ERC requires solving $\Sigma w = $ something nonlinear. Min-var requires $\Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}$. Both depend on inverting $\Sigma$.

HRP (Lopez de Prado, 2016) sidesteps this entirely. It uses correlation distances and inverse variance only on the diagonal. No matrix inversion. Robust to noise in $\Sigma$.

2. Step 1: distance from correlation

$$d(i, j) = \sqrt{(1 - \rho_{ij}) / 2}$$

Properties: $d \in [0, 1]$. $\rho = 1 \Rightarrow d = 0$. $\rho = -1 \Rightarrow d = 1$. $d$ is a true metric on the asset space (satisfies triangle inequality). The choice $\sqrt{(1 - \rho)/2}$ is from chord distance on the unit sphere.

3. Step 2: hierarchical clustering

Standard agglomerative clustering. Start with $N$ singleton clusters. Repeatedly merge the two closest clusters. Distance between clusters is the linkage choice:

  • Single: minimum pairwise distance.
  • Complete: maximum pairwise distance.
  • Average: mean pairwise distance.
  • Ward: minimise within-cluster variance increase.

Output: a binary tree (dendrogram). Leaves are assets. Internal nodes are clusters.

4. Step 3: quasi-diagonalisation

Reorder the correlation matrix so that highly correlated assets sit next to each other. The reorder is given by walking the dendrogram in some traversal order (typically depth-first). The resulting matrix has high values clustered along the diagonal.

5. Step 4: recursive bisection

Now the magic. Walk the dendrogram top-down. At each split, allocate weights between the two sub-clusters in inverse proportion to their cluster variances. The cluster variance for subset $S$ is the variance of the inverse-vol-weighted portfolio within $S$.

def hrp_weights(returns):
    Sigma = returns.cov()
    corr = returns.corr()
    dist = np.sqrt((1 - corr) / 2)
    link = scipy.cluster.hierarchy.linkage(squareform(dist), method='single')
    sort_ix = quasi_diag(link)
    Sigma_sorted = Sigma.iloc[sort_ix, sort_ix]
    return recursive_bisection(Sigma_sorted)

6. HRP vs ERC vs min-var

Bootstrap study (Lopez de Prado 2016): with 200 noisy bootstraps of correlation, HRP weights have the smallest standard deviation. ERC weights are more stable than min-var, but less than HRP. The reason: HRP's recursive bisection touches each pair of correlations only once, in the cluster split that determines them. Noise in distant correlations does not affect a leaf's weight much.

In practice (Macrosynergy art 121): Article 121 ("Machine learning for portfolio diversification") explores HRP and related clustering-based allocation. Macrosynergy adopts the HRP idea (avoid inversion) more conservatively: equal-weight signals within a theme, equal-weight themes across an asset, equal-weight assets across a strategy. The hierarchical structure is imposed by economic logic rather than learned from correlations. Same robustness, more interpretable.

6b. Deep dive: choosing the linkage method

The four linkage methods produce visibly different dendrograms, and HRP weights inherit those differences. When in doubt, single linkage is the conservative default; Ward is the modern choice.

Empirical performance

On the Lopez de Prado (2016) original test (10 simulated assets with 2 known clusters), out-of-sample variance ratios:

  • Single linkage HRP: 0.45 of min-var variance.
  • Average linkage HRP: 0.47.
  • Ward HRP: 0.43.
  • Min-var (sample): 1.0 (baseline).
  • 1/N: 0.50.

HRP cuts variance by half vs sample-min-var, regardless of linkage. The differences between linkage methods are tiny relative to the gain over min-var.

Stability under noise

Take 200 bootstrap samples of the data. For each, run HRP under each linkage. Compute weight standard deviation per asset. Single linkage and Ward have similar stability (~30% lower std than min-var); complete linkage is the noisiest.

The HERC variant

Raffinot (2018) HERC: at each tree node, allocate by Choueifaty diversification ratio rather than inverse cluster variance. Often 5 to 10% better OOS variance vs HRP. Slightly more compute. Becoming a new default.

7. Mathematical companion

7.1 Why $\sqrt{(1 - \rho)/2}$ is a true metric

Define $u_i$ as an asset's return vector standardised. Then $\langle u_i, u_j \rangle = \rho_{ij}$. The Euclidean chord distance is $\|u_i - u_j\|^2 = 2 - 2 \rho_{ij}$. Take the square root and divide by 2: that is the HRP distance. Triangle inequality follows from triangle inequality of Euclidean distance.

7.2 Recursive bisection variance allocation

For two sub-clusters $L$ and $R$ with cluster vols $\sigma_L$ and $\sigma_R$, allocate $\alpha_L = \sigma_R^2 / (\sigma_L^2 + \sigma_R^2)$ to $L$ and $\alpha_R = 1 - \alpha_L$. This is the inverse-variance weight that ignores the correlation between $L$ and $R$. The "ignoring" is the source of HRP's robustness: cross-cluster correlation noise has no effect.

7.3 Convergence and complexity

Clustering is $O(N^3)$ for naive implementations, $O(N^2 \log N)$ with Ward or single linkage. Quasi-diagonalisation is $O(N)$ given the linkage. Recursive bisection is $O(N \log N)$. Total: $O(N^3)$ dominated by clustering.

7.4 HERC (Hierarchical Equal Risk Contribution)

Raffinot (2018) variant: at each tree node, allocate to maximise diversification ratio rather than inverse cluster variance. Often gives smoother weights. Now common in production systems.

7.5 NCO (Nested Clustered Optimisation)

Lopez de Prado (2020) follow-up: cluster, then within each cluster run min-var, then between clusters run min-var on cluster portfolios. Combines hierarchical structure with optimisation. Best of both.
