0. Reading the controls

Universe correlation

The average pairwise correlation. Low correlation = many independent bets, high diversification ratio. High correlation = few bets, low DR.

Vol heterogeneity

The spread of individual asset volatilities. Affects how MDP and ERC differ.

1. The diversification ratio (recap from Lesson 7)

$$DR(w) = \frac{w^\top \sigma}{\sqrt{w^\top \Sigma w}} = \frac{\text{weighted average vol}}{\text{portfolio vol}}$$

Always $\ge 1$. Single asset: DR = 1. Equal weight on $N$ uncorrelated assets: DR $\to \sqrt{N}$. The bigger DR, the more diversification benefit you are extracting.

2. The Maximum Diversification Portfolio

Choueifaty and Coignard (2008) define MDP as the portfolio that maximises DR over the feasible set:

$$w_{\text{MDP}} = \arg\max_{w \in \Delta} \frac{w^\top \sigma}{\sqrt{w^\top \Sigma w}}$$

where $\Delta$ is the simplex (long-only, sum to 1). The MDP has a closed-form-ish solution: it equals the tangency portfolio of a transformed universe where each asset has unit Sharpe (replacing $\mu_i$ with $\sigma_i$ in Markowitz). This makes MDP a cousin of mean-variance, just with $\mu = \sigma$.

Properties of MDP:

  • No expected returns required.
  • Sits in the long-only mean-variance frontier when $\mu_i = \sigma_i$ for all $i$.
  • Often more concentrated than ERC, less than min-var.
  • Sensitive to correlation estimation error.

3. Effective Number of Bets (Meucci 2009)

Meucci proposes a different diversification metric based on the entropy of risk contributions. First, transform the problem to uncorrelated principal components. Then compute the share of total variance from each component:

$$p_k = \frac{\lambda_k (v_k^\top w)^2}{w^\top \Sigma w}$$

where $\lambda_k$ and $v_k$ are eigenvalue/eigenvector pairs. The Effective Number of Bets is

$$\text{ENB} = \exp\left(- \sum_k p_k \log p_k\right)$$

Range: 1 (all risk on one factor) to $N$ (perfect uncorrelated diversification). Unlike DR, ENB is invariant to rotation in the asset space, so it's a more "principled" measure of how many independent bets your portfolio is making.

5. Picking the diversification metric

DR is intuitive (a unit-free ratio) and easy to optimise (closed form). But DR depends on correlation structure in ways that don't always match what investors mean by "diversified".

ENB is more principled (rotation-invariant) but harder to communicate. Useful for risk reporting on factor-rich portfolios.

Effective N from RC entropy (Lesson 7) is similar to ENB but works in the original asset space. Easier to communicate, less rotation-invariant.

Production practice: compute DR and effective-N for monitoring; use ERC or HRP as the actual allocation method (Lessons 14, 15). MDP is rare in production because of correlation-error sensitivity.

In practice (Macrosynergy art 3): The flagship cross-asset relative-value strategy (art 3) is built on equal-weighted scorecards. The empirical result: it has DR around 2.5 and ENB around 5 across 50+ instruments. MS does not run MDP because the optimised correlation structure is not stable enough. Instead they get diversification by combining many uncorrelated signals (theoretical priors), which maps each signal to roughly equal risk regardless of estimated correlations.

5b. Deep dive: when do allocations agree and when do they diverge?

Equal weight, ERC, MDP, and minimum variance can all give similar weights or wildly different ones depending on the universe. Understanding the regimes makes you a smarter allocator.

Special cases where they coincide

  • Identical vols, identical correlations. All four = $1/N$. Symmetry forces uniform weights.
  • Identical correlations, heterogeneous vols. ERC = MDP = inverse-vol weights. Min-var heavily overweights the lowest-vol asset; equal weight ignores the differences.
  • Diagonal $\Sigma$ (uncorrelated assets), equal vol. All four = $1/N$.

Cases where they diverge dramatically

  • One very low-vol asset (bonds at 5%) + several high-vol assets. Min-var puts 60 to 80% in the bond. ERC puts ~25 to 35%. MDP somewhere in between. Equal weight ignores it.
  • Cluster structure (3 cluster of correlated assets). Min-var concentrates in low-correlation orphans. ERC spreads roughly equally. MDP picks within-cluster diversifiers.
  • One asset with negative correlation to the rest. Min-var massively overweights it (sometimes >100%). ERC and MDP modestly overweight.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 MDP closed-form via the tangency analogy

Treat each asset as having $\hat \mu_i = \sigma_i$. Then the tangency portfolio formula gives

$$w_{\text{MDP}} \propto \Sigma^{-1} \sigma$$

where $\sigma$ is the vector of asset volatilities. Normalise to sum to 1.

6.2 ENB rotation invariance

The principal component decomposition $\Sigma = V \Lambda V^\top$ is unique up to sign. The shares $p_k = \lambda_k (v_k^\top w)^2 / (w^\top \Sigma w)$ are invariant to relabelling of components. So ENB does not depend on basis choice.

6.3 Why DR is bounded above by $\sqrt{N}$ in the uncorrelated case

For uncorrelated assets with equal vol, equal weight gives portfolio vol $\sigma / \sqrt{N}$. Weighted average vol stays at $\sigma$. Ratio: $\sqrt{N}$. Adding correlation pulls portfolio vol up (toward $\sigma$), reducing DR.

6.4 The MDP is the "1/sigma" portfolio for uncorrelated assets

If $\Sigma$ is diagonal, $\Sigma^{-1} \sigma$ becomes $\sigma / \sigma_i^2 = 1 / \sigma_i$. The MDP is exactly inverse-volatility weighting. Adding correlations rotates the allocation away from $1 / \sigma_i$.

6.5 Effective N from risk contributions

Define $r_i = \text{RC}_i / \sigma_p$ as the share of total risk from asset $i$. Then effective N $= \exp(-\sum_i r_i \log r_i)$. ERC has effective N $= N$ exactly. Concentrated portfolios have effective N near 1.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: MDP closed form

def mdp(Sigma):
    sigmas = np.sqrt(np.diag(Sigma))
    w = np.linalg.solve(Sigma, sigmas)
    return w / w.sum()

Exercise B: ENB from PCA

def enb(w, Sigma):
    eigvals, eigvecs = np.linalg.eigh(Sigma)
    p = eigvals * (eigvecs.T @ w) ** 2 / (w @ Sigma @ w)
    p = p[p > 1e-12]
    return np.exp(-(p * np.log(p)).sum())
