0. Reading the controls

Number of regimes

2 (calm, stress) is most common. 3 (calm, normal, stress) is the next step. More than 3 rarely justifies the added complexity.

Smoothing parameter

How responsive the regime classifier is. High = fast switching. Low = sticky regimes.

Stress allocation tilt

How much to deleverage in stress regime. Common: 50 to 80% of normal exposure.

1. What is a regime?

A regime is a state of the world where the joint distribution of returns differs systematically from another state. Examples:

  • Risk-on / risk-off. Equity-bond correlation, vol levels.
  • Inflation regime. CPI growth above or below central bank target.
  • Growth regime. Real GDP growth above or below trend.
  • Monetary regime. Easing or tightening cycles.

The economic content matters. A regime is useful only if it predicts something different from the prior. "Crisis" is a regime; "Tuesday" is not.

2. Hidden Markov Models

Hamilton (1989) brought HMMs to macro. The idea: returns depend on a hidden state $s_t$ that switches according to a Markov chain. The state is unobservable; we infer probabilities via Baum-Welch.

$$P(s_t = j \mid s_{t-1} = i) = T_{ij}, \quad r_t \mid s_t = j \sim N(\mu_j, \Sigma_j)$$

Output: filtered probability $P(s_t = j \mid r_{1:t})$ at each time. Use as a soft tilt on allocation rather than a hard switch.

3. Regime classifiers from macro features

Alternative to HMM: build a classifier from observable macro features.

  • VIX level + 200d MA → vol regime.
  • Yield curve slope + ISM PMI → growth regime.
  • CPI surprise + breakeven inflation → inflation regime.

Macrosynergy uses random forests (art 111, 136) to combine many macro features into a regime probability. Trained on historical regimes labelled by drawdown depth or NBER recession.

4. Regime-conditional allocation

Given regime probabilities $\pi_t = (\pi_{1t}, \ldots, \pi_{Kt})$ and per-regime target weights $w^{(k)}$:

$$w_t = \sum_k \pi_{kt} \cdot w^{(k)}$$

The portfolio smoothly blends the per-regime targets weighted by current probabilities.

Per-regime targets are calibrated to the expected return distribution in each regime. Stress regime: defensive (more bonds, gold, less equity). Calm regime: offensive (more equity, EM, less duration).

5. Smooth tilting vs hard switching

Hard switching: pick the most likely regime, allocate accordingly. Cliff edges. High turnover at regime boundaries.

Smooth tilting: weight by probability. Continuous transitions. Lower turnover. Production preference.

Empirical: smooth tilting beats hard switching by 0.05 to 0.15 Sharpe in most studies, mostly from reduced turnover and less whipsawing.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 The Baum-Welch algorithm

EM algorithm for HMMs. Iterate:

  1. E-step: compute filtered and smoothed regime probabilities given current parameters.
  2. M-step: re-estimate transition matrix $T$ and emission distributions $\{\mu_j, \Sigma_j\}$ from probability-weighted data.

Converges to local optimum of the likelihood. Multiple random starts help find the global one.

6.2 The forward-backward filter

Filtered probability: $\alpha_t(j) = P(s_t = j \mid r_{1:t})$.

Smoothed: $\gamma_t(j) = P(s_t = j \mid r_{1:T})$.

Use filtered for real-time; use smoothed for historical analysis (don't peek).

6.3 Regime-conditional vs unconditional Sharpe

If regime $k$ has Sharpe $\text{SR}_k$ and probability $\pi_k$ unconditionally:

$$\text{SR}_{\text{static}} \le \text{SR}_{\text{regime-aware}}$$

The gap depends on how different the regimes are. For 2 regimes with very different conditional means, the gap is large.

6.4 Why hard switching whipsaws

When the regime probability hovers near 0.5, hard switching produces noisy decisions. Smooth tilting averages them out.

6.5 Persistent vs transient regimes

If $T_{ii} \approx 0.99$ (regime persists with prob 99% per day), expected duration is 100 days. Calibrate $T$ to historical regime length to avoid spurious switches.

8. Self test

Question 1, conceptual

Why is smooth tilting preferable to hard switching at regime boundaries?

Question 2, coding

Modify Exercise A to use 3 states (calm, normal, stress). Compare conditional Sharpe to 2-state.

Question 3, numerical

If $T_{ii} = 0.95$ daily, what is expected regime duration in trading days?

Answer: $1 / (1 - 0.95) = 20$ days.
