0. Reading the controls

Confidence $\alpha$

Same as Lesson 3. CVaR$_\alpha$ averages losses past the $\alpha$-quantile. Common values: 0.95, 0.99.

Drawdown threshold

The MinMax framework scales leverage when rolling drawdown exceeds this. Typical: 10% to 15%.

Aversion parameter

Controls how aggressively to deleverage when drawdown exceeds the threshold. Higher = more conservative.

1. Recap

Variance is symmetric and Gaussian-flavoured. CVaR captures left-tail expectation. Drawdown captures path dependence (peak-to-trough). Different objectives, different optima.

The RU formulation makes CVaR optimisation as fast as mean-variance. The MinMax framework adapts drawdown control without requiring forecasts.

2. The Rockafellar-Uryasev LP

Rockafellar and Uryasev (2000) showed that for empirical scenarios $r_1, \ldots, r_T$:

$$\text{CVaR}_\alpha(L) = \min_{c} \left[ c + \frac{1}{(1 - \alpha) T} \sum_{t=1}^T \max(0, L_t - c) \right]$$

The $\max(0, \cdot)$ is the loss past threshold $c$. Linearise with slack variables $\eta_t \ge L_t - c$, $\eta_t \ge 0$. Then mean-CVaR optimisation becomes a linear program:

$$\min_{w, c, \eta} c + \frac{1}{(1 - \alpha) T} \sum_t \eta_t$$

subject to $\eta_t \ge -w^\top r_t - c$, $\eta_t \ge 0$, $\sum w = 1$, $w^\top \bar r \ge \mu_{\text{target}}$.

This is Lesson 3 risk measures meet Lesson 6 KKT. Same convex optimisation machinery, different objective.

3. Drawdown optimisation

Maximum drawdown $\text{MDD}(W) = \max_t (1 - W_t / \max_{s \le t} W_s)$ is a path-dependent functional. Direct optimisation is non-convex.

Conditional Drawdown at Risk (CDaR, Chekhlov-Uryasev-Zabarankin 2005) is the average drawdown across the worst $1 - \alpha$ fraction of the path. CDaR has a similar LP formulation to CVaR.

For practical purposes, the MinMax framework (next section) is more common.

4. MinMax drawdown control

Macrosynergy art 220 introduces a simple but effective drawdown overlay. Compute the rolling drawdown $D_t$ on the strategy. Scale portfolio leverage by:

$$L_t = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } D_t < D^\star \\ \exp\left(-\kappa (D_t - D^\star)\right) & \text{if } D_t \ge D^\star \end{cases}$$

where $D^\star$ is the drawdown threshold (e.g. 10%) and $\kappa$ is the aversion parameter. Properties:

  • Above threshold: leverage decays exponentially with drawdown depth.
  • Below threshold: full leverage.
  • No expected-return forecast required.
  • Recovers smoothly when drawdown shrinks.

5. Stress testing

The bridge between optimisation and risk management. Once you have a portfolio, replay it through historical stress periods (2008, 2020, 2022). Report drawdowns and recoveries. The portfolio should survive each stress scenario at the level of risk you set.

For CVaR-optimised portfolios under historical scenarios, the realised CVaR in stress matches the optimised CVaR (by construction of the LP). For MinMax drawdown control, the realised max drawdown is bounded but not zero: deleveraging cuts losses, doesn't eliminate them.

In practice (Macrosynergy art 220 + every other strategy): The MS production stack for any strategy is volatility targeting (Lesson 21) + MinMax drawdown control (this lesson). CVaR is reserved for academic comparison and stress reporting. The reason: vol targets and drawdown stops respond fast (within a week of regime shift). Parametric CVaR depends on a fixed loss distribution that is wrong during regime transitions.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Why the RU LP works

$\text{CVaR}_\alpha(L) = \min_c \mathbb{E}[c + \tfrac{1}{1-\alpha}(L - c)^+]$. Take the empirical version with $T$ scenarios, replace $\mathbb{E}$ by sample average. The $(L - c)^+$ pieces become $\eta_t = \max(0, L_t - c)$. Standard linearisation puts $\eta_t \ge L_t - c$, $\eta_t \ge 0$ and minimises $c + \tfrac{1}{(1-\alpha)T} \sum \eta_t$.

6.2 Convexity of CVaR

VaR is non-convex (it's a quantile). CVaR is the upper Lagrangian envelope of VaR over the dual measures (Lesson 3). Sup over linear functionals is convex, so CVaR is convex. This is what allows the LP formulation.

6.3 CDaR LP formulation

$\text{CDaR}_\alpha = \min_c \tfrac{1}{(1-\alpha)T} \sum_t \max(0, D_t - c) + c$. Same structure as CVaR LP but $D_t$ is the rolling drawdown of the equity curve at time $t$.

6.4 The exponential leverage rule of MinMax

The exponential decay $L_t = \exp(-\kappa (D_t - D^\star))$ has two desirable properties: smooth (differentiable in $D_t$, no discontinuities) and fast-recovering (when drawdown shrinks, leverage rises automatically). Linear or step functions also work but with worse practical behaviour.

6.5 Why MinMax does not need expected returns

The leverage rule depends only on realised drawdown, not on expected return. This is the central insight: drawdown control is a function of the past, not of forecasts. It separates risk management from alpha forecasting.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: Mean-CVaR LP

import cvxpy as cp
def mean_cvar(returns, alpha=0.95, mu_target=None):
    T, n = returns.shape
    w = cp.Variable(n)
    c = cp.Variable()
    eta = cp.Variable(T, nonneg=True)
    losses = -returns @ w
    obj = cp.Minimize(c + cp.sum(eta) / ((1 - alpha) * T))
    cons = [eta >= losses - c, cp.sum(w) == 1, w >= 0]
    if mu_target is not None:
        cons.append(returns.mean(axis=0) @ w >= mu_target)
    cp.Problem(obj, cons).solve()
    return w.value

Exercise C: MinMax drawdown overlay

def minmax_overlay(strategy_returns, threshold=0.10, kappa=15):
    eq = (1 + strategy_returns).cumprod()
    peak = np.maximum.accumulate(eq)
    dd = 1 - eq / peak
    leverage = np.where(dd < threshold, 1.0, np.exp(-kappa * (dd - threshold)))
    return strategy_returns * leverage

8. Self test

Question 1, conceptual

Why is CVaR convex while VaR is not? Use the dual representation of CVaR as a sup over distortion measures.

Question 2, coding

Modify the MinMax overlay to also impose a maximum leverage cap (e.g. cannot exceed 1.5x).

Question 3, numerical

For an equity curve with 20% max drawdown, $D^\star = 10\%$, $\kappa = 15$, what is the leverage at the trough?

Answer: $L = \exp(-15 \times 0.10) = \exp(-1.5) \approx 0.22$. The portfolio is deleveraged to 22% of normal at the trough.
