0. Reading the controls

Market cap weights

The starting point. BL assumes the market portfolio is held by a representative investor in equilibrium. Realistic market caps for global asset classes: equity 60%, bonds 30%, alternatives 10%, with sub-allocations.

Risk aversion $\lambda$

The implicit risk aversion of the representative market investor. Default: $\lambda = 2.5$, calibrated to historical equity Sharpe.

$\tau$

The "prior strength" parameter. Smaller $\tau$ means a tighter prior on equilibrium returns. Common values: 0.025 (one over the sample size, $\approx 1/T$) to 0.05.

View return and confidence

For each view, the expected return and the confidence (low, medium, high). High confidence means low $\Omega$, so the view dominates the prior. Low confidence means high $\Omega$, so the prior dominates.

1. Reverse optimisation: the equilibrium prior

Markowitz tells us how to compute optimal weights given $\mu$ and $\Sigma$. Reverse it: given that the market holds weights $w_m$, what $\mu$ would make this optimal?

$$\Pi = \lambda \Sigma w_m$$

$\Pi$ is the vector of "implied equilibrium returns". This is the prior in BL. It is automatically internally consistent: if you optimise on $\Pi$ you get back $w_m$. No estimation, just algebra.

2. Encoding views

A view in BL is a linear belief about the assets: $P \mu = Q + \epsilon$, $\epsilon \sim N(0, \Omega)$. Two flavours:

Absolute view. "I think US equities will return 5%". $P = (1, 0, 0, \ldots)$, $Q = 0.05$.

Relative view. "I think Europe will outperform US by 2%". $P = (-1, 1, 0, \ldots)$, $Q = 0.02$.

$\Omega$ is the diagonal matrix of view variances. Smaller diagonal entry means higher confidence. Typically calibrated as $\Omega_{kk} = \tau \cdot p_k^\top \Sigma p_k$ scaled by a confidence multiplier.

3. The master formula

The posterior mean of $\mu$ given the equilibrium prior and views is

$$\mu_{BL} = \left[(\tau \Sigma)^{-1} + P^\top \Omega^{-1} P\right]^{-1} \left[(\tau \Sigma)^{-1} \Pi + P^\top \Omega^{-1} Q\right]$$

The structure is: combine two precision-weighted means. The equilibrium prior contributes $(\tau \Sigma)^{-1} \Pi$. The views contribute $P^\top \Omega^{-1} Q$. The denominator is the combined precision.

Plug $\mu_{BL}$ back into Markowitz to get the BL portfolio. The result has weights that are tilts away from $w_m$, in the direction of the views, scaled by view confidence.

4. $\tau$, $\Omega$, and confidence calibration

$\tau$ scales the entire prior covariance. Smaller $\tau$ = stronger prior. Empirical default: $\tau = 1/T$ where $T$ is your effective sample size.

$\Omega$ encodes view confidence. Three popular calibrations:

  • Idzorek (2005): specify confidence as a percent (0 to 100%) and back out $\Omega$ such that the posterior weight shift matches.
  • $\Omega = \tau P \Sigma P^\top$: set view variances proportional to the prior. Self-scaling.
  • Manual: directly set $\Omega_{kk}$ in basis points squared. Most flexible, requires intuition.

5. Practical pitfalls

Inconsistent views. If two views are roughly contradictory (e.g. "US up 5%" and "global equities flat"), $P^\top \Omega^{-1} P$ may be ill-conditioned. Orthogonalise views before encoding.

Posterior covariance not always tighter. Some BL implementations forget to update $\Sigma$ as well. Default: $\Sigma_{BL} = \Sigma + ((\tau \Sigma)^{-1} + P^\top \Omega^{-1} P)^{-1}$.

Sensitivity to $\lambda$. The implied equilibrium returns scale linearly with $\lambda$. Pick $\lambda$ from historical Sharpe (typically 2 to 4 for global equity). Document your choice.

In practice (Macrosynergy): Macrosynergy does not use Black-Litterman directly. Their philosophical equivalent is conceptual parity: pin weights by economic justification (each macro factor gets equal risk budget), do not let the data move them. BL is closer to the "tactical asset allocation" practice at large institutions (Goldman, BlackRock, AQR for some products). Where MS sits is more like "build your alpha from macro signals, then equal-weight, never optimise". A useful synthesis: use BL to combine an equilibrium prior with a Macrosynergy macro signal as the view. The BL posterior gives you mean-shifts that respect both.

5b. Deep dive: encoding views in the wild

Translating qualitative views to BL inputs is where most BL implementations get sloppy. Four worked examples.

Example 1: simple absolute view

"US equities will return 7% annually" with high confidence.

$P = (1, 0, 0, 0, 0)$, $Q = 0.07$, $\Omega = \tau \cdot P \Sigma P^\top \cdot \theta$ where $\theta < 1$ is a confidence multiplier. For high confidence $\theta = 0.1$: $\Omega \approx 0.0008$ on the diagonal. Posterior US equity return moves close to 7%.

Example 2: relative view

"European equities outperform US equities by 2%" with medium confidence.

$P = (-1, 1, 0, 0, 0)$, $Q = 0.02$. The view is on the spread, not the absolute level. With medium confidence ($\theta = 1$), the spread shifts toward 2% but absolute levels barely move from equilibrium.

Example 3: portfolio view (cross-sectional rank)

"Top quartile of value stocks outperforms bottom quartile by 4%". $P$ has +1/N in the top quartile rows, -1/N in the bottom, zero elsewhere. $Q = 0.04$. This encodes a classic value strategy.

Example 4: hedged view

"US equities up 5%, but I want to express this without taking sector risk". $P$ is a beta-neutral or sector-neutral combination. The Idzorek (2005) extension shows how to encode "I want to bet $w$% conditional on the view materialising".

The three traps

Trap 1. Forgetting to specify $\tau$. Default $\tau = 1/T$ where $T$ is your effective sample size. Common values 0.025 (40 years of monthly) to 0.10 (30 months).

Trap 2. Using too many views. With $N$ assets and $K$ views, the effective dimension reduction goes from $N$ to $N - K$. Past 10 views, you are essentially specifying $\mu$ directly.

Trap 3. Inconsistent views. Two views that contradict produce posterior chaos. Always check that $P^\top \Omega^{-1} P$ has reasonable condition number.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 The reverse optimisation derivation

Mean-variance solution: $w^\star = (\lambda \Sigma)^{-1} \mu$. Set $w^\star = w_m$ and solve for $\mu$: $\mu = \lambda \Sigma w_m = \Pi$. Done.

6.2 The master formula derivation

Treat $\mu \sim N(\Pi, \tau \Sigma)$ as the prior. The view equation $P \mu = Q$ has likelihood $N(P\mu, \Omega)$. Combine via Bayes' rule. The posterior mean is the precision-weighted average.

6.3 Why $\tau \Sigma$ instead of $\Sigma$ as the prior covariance

$\Sigma$ is the variance of returns, not of expected returns. The variance of the expected return is much smaller; we use $\tau \Sigma$ as a parametric guess at how uncertain we are about $\mu$. $\tau$ encodes "how many years of data is the prior worth" inversely.

6.4 The Idzorek confidence-to-$\Omega$ calibration

Pick a confidence $\alpha \in [0, 1]$. Set $\Omega$ such that the resulting posterior weight on view $k$'s "100% confidence" implementation matches a fraction $\alpha$ of the way from equilibrium to "100% view" weights. Closed form in Idzorek (2005).

6.5 Connection to Bayesian linear models

BL is exactly Bayesian linear regression with a normal-normal-normal model. The "data" is the views, the "parameter" is $\mu$, the "prior" is equilibrium. Standard conjugate update.

def equilibrium_returns(w_m, Sigma, lam=2.5):

Exercise B: Master formula

def black_litterman(Pi, Sigma, P, Q, Omega, tau=0.05):
    tau_Sigma_inv = np.linalg.inv(tau * Sigma)
    Omega_inv = np.linalg.inv(Omega)
    A = tau_Sigma_inv + P.T @ Omega_inv @ P
    b = tau_Sigma_inv @ Pi + P.T @ Omega_inv @ Q
    return np.linalg.solve(A, b)

8. Self test

Question 1, conceptual

Why does BL avoid the error maximisation problem of plain Markowitz?

Answer: The equilibrium prior anchors the optimisation. Without strong views, the BL portfolio is close to the market. Views shift the portfolio away from the market only in proportion to confidence. Estimation noise affects the prior weakly because the prior is constructed analytically (not estimated).

Question 2, coding

Implement the full BL pipeline (reverse opt + master formula + Markowitz on the posterior). Test on a 3-asset universe with one absolute view at high confidence. Verify the posterior weight on the view asset moves toward the view's implied weight.

Question 3, numerical

For $\tau = 0.05$, $\Omega = 0.02^2$, view return 5%, equilibrium return 6%, what is the posterior return? Use the 1D version of the master formula.
