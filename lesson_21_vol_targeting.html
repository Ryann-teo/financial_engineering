0. Reading the controls

Target volatility $\sigma_{\text{target}}$

Annualised. Common institutional ranges: 6 to 8% for low-risk products, 10 to 12% for typical multi-strategy, 15 to 20% for aggressive.

EWMA half-life

How fast the vol estimator forgets old data. Macrosynergy default: 11 trading days. RiskMetrics standard: ~30 days. Shorter = more reactive but noisier.

Maximum leverage cap

Hard ceiling on the leverage multiplier. Prevents runaway leverage when vol falls below normal. Typical: 3x.

1. The mechanic

Vol targeting is dead simple:

$$w_t = \min\left(L_{\max}, \frac{\sigma_{\text{target}}}{\hat\sigma_t}\right) \cdot w_t^{\text{signal}}$$

where $\hat \sigma_t$ is your forecast of next period's volatility and $w_t^{\text{signal}}$ is your unscaled signal weight. Computed each rebalancing period, applied to every asset (or to the portfolio as a whole).

2. EWMA volatility estimation

$$\hat\sigma_t^2 = \alpha r_{t-1}^2 + (1 - \alpha) \hat\sigma_{t-1}^2$$

where $\alpha = 1 - 0.5^{1/h}$ for half-life $h$. The forecaster gives more weight to recent observations, with weights decaying geometrically.

Critical: $\hat \sigma_t$ uses information up to time $t-1$. No look-ahead bias allowed.

3. Why vol targeting helps

Vol targeting cuts position size before drawdowns deepen and increases position size when vol is low. Empirically:

  • Drawdowns shrink. Volatility tends to spike at the start of crashes; vol targeting de-leverages quickly.
  • Sharpe usually improves. Moreira and Muir (2017) document Sharpe gains of 0.1 to 0.3 across asset classes from simple vol targeting.
  • Tail risk drops. By construction, the vol-targeted portfolio has more stable risk over time, reducing kurtosis.

The mechanism is an "automatic stop-loss": when realised vol jumps, leverage drops. When vol normalises, leverage returns.

4. Half-life choice

Three regimes:

  • Short (5 to 15 days). Reactive. Catches vol spikes early. High turnover. Macrosynergy default at 11 days.
  • Medium (20 to 40 days). RiskMetrics default. Balance of responsiveness and stability. Most production systems.
  • Long (60+ days). Slow. Misses fast regime changes but uses less data. Some pension funds.

Empirically, short half-lives win in markets with frequent vol regime shifts (post-2008 equity, crypto). Long half-lives win in stable markets. Test on your data.

5. Interaction with momentum

AQR (Time Series Momentum, 2012) and Macrosynergy show that vol targeting AND momentum is greater than the sum of parts. Reasons:

  • Momentum positions go long after low-vol up-trending periods, so vol-targeting up-leverages winners.
  • Momentum positions go short or flat in high-vol crashes, so vol-targeting de-leverages just before drawdowns.
  • The combination has positive skew (trend captures upside; vol target avoids tail).
In practice (Macrosynergy art 5, 245): Article 5 (FX with point-in-time GDP growth) uses 11-day EWMA for vol estimation as the production default. Article 245 ("Volatility-targeted equity allocation") quantifies the effect: SPY with 10% vol target produces 25% Sharpe improvement and 35 to 50% tail risk reduction over the unscaled equivalent. The MS production stack always combines vol targeting with drawdown control (Lesson 16); both layers respond to realised stress.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 The "vol drag" misconception

Vol drag is not the reason vol targeting works. The geometric mean is roughly $\mu - \sigma^2/2$. Targeting fixed vol by changing leverage does not eliminate this. The Sharpe gain from vol targeting comes from negative autocorrelation in volatility levels (calm markets stay calm, stressed markets stay stressed) and the asymmetric impact of vol on returns.

6.2 The Moreira-Muir Sharpe-improvement formula

If realised vol has autocorrelation $\rho_v$, vol targeting Sharpe gain is approximately

$$\Delta \text{SR} \approx \rho_v \cdot \text{SR}_{\text{base}} \cdot \nu$$

where $\nu$ is the relative vol of vol. Empirically $\rho_v \approx 0.7$ on equity indices and $\nu \approx 0.3$, giving 20 to 30% Sharpe improvement.

6.3 EWMA mean-reverting properties

EWMA volatility is technically integrated GARCH (no constant). Its long-run forecast is undefined; it relies on the most recent shocks. For multi-day forecasts, the EWMA forecast is just $\sigma_t$ for all horizons. Honest but limited.

6.4 The "look-ahead trap"

Many naive vol-target backtests use $\hat\sigma_t$ computed using $r_t$ in the same period. This produces fake Sharpe gains. Always lag the vol estimate: scale today's position by yesterday's vol.

6.5 Vol targeting as a regularizer

From an optimisation viewpoint, vol targeting is a constraint $\sqrt{w^\top \Sigma_t w} = \sigma_{\text{target}}$. Combined with mean-variance, it removes one degree of freedom (the magnitude of $w$). The portfolio direction is mean-variance optimal; the magnitude is set by vol target.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: EWMA vol forecaster

def ewma_vol(returns: pd.Series, half_life: float = 22) -> pd.Series:
    alpha = 1 - 0.5 ** (1 / half_life)
    return returns.ewm(alpha=alpha).std() * np.sqrt(252)

Exercise B: Apply vol targeting

def vol_target(returns: pd.Series, target_vol: float = 0.10, half_life: int = 22, max_lev: float = 3.0):
    sigma_t = ewma_vol(returns, half_life).shift(1)
    leverage = (target_vol / sigma_t).clip(upper=max_lev).fillna(1.0)
    return leverage * returns
