0. Reading the controls

Skew preference

Whether you prefer positive or negative skew. Investors prefer positive skew (lottery-like), so positive-skew assets command lower expected returns. Conversely negative-skew assets pay a "skew premium".

Copula family

Gaussian (no tail dependence), t (symmetric tail dependence), Clayton (lower-tail only), Gumbel (upper-tail only). Choose to match the empirical co-crash behaviour you see.

Tail dependence parameter

For the t copula, df controls tail dependence: low df = strong joint tails. For Clayton, $\theta$ controls lower-tail dependence.

1. Mean-variance-skew-kurtosis

Generalise mean-variance utility to include higher moments:

$$U(w) = \mu_p - \tfrac{\lambda_2}{2} \sigma_p^2 + \tfrac{\lambda_3}{6} S_p - \tfrac{\lambda_4}{24} K_p$$

where $\mu_p, \sigma_p^2, S_p, K_p$ are portfolio mean, variance, third central moment, fourth central moment. The signs reflect typical investor preferences: like upside skew, dislike fat tails.

Computing $S_p$ requires the co-skewness tensor $C_{ijk} = \mathbb{E}[(r_i - \mu_i)(r_j - \mu_j)(r_k - \mu_k)]$. Computing $K_p$ requires the co-kurtosis tensor $D_{ijkl}$. Both grow as $N^3$ and $N^4$ in size, making direct optimisation hard for large $N$.

2. Polynomial Goal Programming

Lai (1991) sidesteps utility function specification by treating each moment as a goal:

$$\min_w \sum_k \alpha_k \left| \frac{m_k(w) - m_k^*}{m_k^*} \right|^{p_k}$$

where $m_k^*$ is the achievable optimum on moment $k$ alone. Choose $\alpha_k$ by preference. Tractable, and avoids the philosophical mess of choosing a utility function.

3. Copulas: dependence beyond correlation

Sklar's theorem: any joint distribution $F(x_1, \ldots, x_N)$ decomposes uniquely into marginals plus a copula:

$$F(x_1, \ldots, x_N) = C(F_1(x_1), \ldots, F_N(x_N))$$

The copula $C$ captures all dependence; the marginals $F_i$ capture all univariate distribution shape.

Why this matters: two distributions can have identical correlation matrices but very different joint tail behaviour. The Gaussian copula has zero tail dependence: extreme co-movements are vanishingly rare. The Student t copula has symmetric tail dependence: crashes and rallies cluster together. Clayton has lower-tail dependence only: crashes cluster, rallies don't.

4. Tail dependence coefficients

The lower-tail dependence coefficient is

$$\lambda_L = \lim_{u \to 0^+} P(F_1(X_1) \le u \mid F_2(X_2) \le u)$$

If $\lambda_L > 0$, asset 1 crashing makes asset 2's crash much more likely. Examples:

  • Gaussian: $\lambda_L = 0$. No tail dependence.
  • Student t (df = 4, $\rho = 0.5$): $\lambda_L \approx 0.30$.
  • Clayton ($\theta = 2$): $\lambda_L = 2^{-1/2} \approx 0.71$.

For risk management, the t and Clayton copulas paint a darker picture than Gaussian. The same $\rho = 0.5$ produces 0% co-crash under Gaussian and 30%+ under Student t with df = 4.

5. Multivariate t portfolios

Replace the multivariate normal with multivariate t in your portfolio model. The variance is no longer the right risk measure; CVaR or tail-VaR is. Optimisation under multivariate t can be done via Monte Carlo or with closed forms for elliptical distributions.

In practice (Macrosynergy art 96, 300): Article 96 ("Copulas and trading strategies") demonstrates copulas for cross-asset carry. Article 300 on cross-asset carry copulas shows that the diversification benefits of cross-asset carry are smaller than Gaussian models predict because tail dependence rises during crises. Macrosynergy uses copulas mainly for stress testing and tail-risk attribution, not for optimisation. The production stack still uses vol targeting + drawdown control (Lessons 21, 16) because they react fast to realised tail clustering.

Empirical $\lambda_L$ for asset pairs

  • SPY-EFA (US-International equity): $\lambda_L \approx 0.45$, much higher than the Pearson correlation 0.65 would suggest under Gaussian.
  • SPY-AGG (equity-bond): $\lambda_L \approx 0.05$ in calm regimes, jumps to 0.30 in 2008-style stress. Time-varying.
  • EM equity pairs: $\lambda_L = 0.50$ to 0.65. Crashes co-cluster strongly.
  • Crypto pairs (BTC-ETH): $\lambda_L \approx 0.70$. Almost always crash together.

Implications for portfolio construction

Diversification under heavy joint tails is less effective than under Gaussian. A "diversified" portfolio of US, EM, commodities under Gaussian assumptions might produce 99% VaR of 4%; under realistic heavy-tail copulas, the same portfolio's 99% VaR is closer to 6 to 7%. The implications:

  1. Always stress-test portfolios under non-Gaussian dependence.
  2. Use CVaR rather than variance as the optimisation objective.
  3. Apply MinMax drawdown control as a runtime safety net (Lesson 16).
  4. Monitor realised tail dependence during stress to detect "diversification breakdown".

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Why correlation under-tells the joint risk

Correlation is a moment of order 2. Tail dependence is a property of order $\infty$ (limit of conditional probabilities). They can disagree dramatically. Two distributions with identical covariance can have $\lambda_L = 0$ (Gaussian) and $\lambda_L = 0.7$ (Clayton).

6.2 Sklar's theorem proof sketch

Define $U_i = F_i(X_i)$. Then $U_i \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ (probability integral transform). Joint distribution of $(U_1, \ldots, U_N)$ is the copula by definition. Marginals are uniform. Done.

6.3 Computing $\lambda_L$ for Student t copula

For bivariate t with correlation $\rho$ and df $\nu$:

$$\lambda_L = 2 \cdot t_{\nu+1}\left(-\sqrt{\frac{(\nu+1)(1-\rho)}{1+\rho}}\right)$$

where $t_{\nu+1}$ is the CDF of a univariate t with $\nu+1$ df. Closed form, no simulation needed.

6.4 Polynomial Goal Programming guarantees

Convex if each $|m_k(w) - m_k^*|^{p_k}$ is convex. Mean and variance are convex in $w$ for any $\Sigma$ PSD. Skew and kurtosis are not generally convex. For practical use, linearise around the current $w$ and iterate.

6.5 Co-skewness tensor explosion

Co-skewness has $N(N+1)(N+2)/6$ unique entries. For $N = 50$ that's 22,100. Estimating each from finite data is hopeless without parametric assumptions. Standard practice: assume an elliptical distribution (no co-skewness) or use a factor-model decomposition to keep parameter count low.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: Simulate from a Student t copula

def sample_t_copula(rho, df, n=1000, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    Sigma = np.array([[1, rho], [rho, 1]])
    L = np.linalg.cholesky(Sigma)
    z = rng.standard_normal((n, 2)) @ L.T
    chi = rng.chisquare(df, n)
    t = z / np.sqrt(chi[:, None] / df)
    # Convert to uniform via t CDF (probability integral transform)
    from scipy.stats import t as tdist
    return tdist.cdf(t, df)

Exercise B: Estimate $\lambda_L$ from data

def empirical_tail_dep(returns_df, level=0.05):
    n = len(returns_df)
    qs = returns_df.quantile(level)
    both_low = (returns_df.iloc[:, 0] < qs[0]) & (returns_df.iloc[:, 1] < qs[1])
    return both_low.sum() / (level * n)
