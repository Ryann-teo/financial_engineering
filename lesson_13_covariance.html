0. Reading the controls

Estimator type

Six choices: sample, EWMA, Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage, single-factor, RMT-cleaned, hierarchical block. Each has different bias-variance trade-offs.

Sample size T, universe size N

The cliff at $T \approx N$ is real. RMT cleaning is most useful when $T/N < 5$. Sample covariance works for $T/N > 30$.

EWMA half-life

Shorter = more responsive to recent regime, less stable. RiskMetrics standard: ~30-day half-life on daily data.

1. The six estimators

  1. Sample. $S = (X - \bar X)^\top (X - \bar X) / (T - 1)$. Unbiased but noisy.
  2. EWMA. $S_t = \alpha r_{t-1} r_{t-1}^\top + (1 - \alpha) S_{t-1}$. Time-varying, responsive.
  3. Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage. Lesson 10. Linear blend with structured target.
  4. Single-factor. $\Sigma = \beta \beta^\top \sigma_m^2 + D$. Parsimonious; $D$ is the diagonal of residuals.
  5. RMT-cleaned. Eigenvalue clipping. Replace noise eigenvalues with their average.
  6. Hierarchical block. Cluster assets into groups; within-block correlations preserved, between-block shrunk.

2. Random Matrix Theory cleaning

Sample covariance eigenvalues that fall inside the Marcenko-Pastur interval $[\lambda_-, \lambda_+]$ are pure noise. Replace them with their average:

$$\hat \lambda_i = \begin{cases} \lambda_i & \text{if outside MP interval} \\ \bar \lambda_{\text{noise}} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Reconstruct $\hat \Sigma = V \hat \Lambda V^\top$. The result has the same trace as the original (variance preserved) but is much better conditioned. Laloux et al. (2000) introduced this for finance.

3. Factor model covariance

Decompose $\Sigma$ as a low-rank factor part plus an idiosyncratic diagonal:

$$\Sigma = B \Omega_F B^\top + D$$

$B$ is $N \times K$ factor loadings, $\Omega_F$ is $K \times K$ factor covariance, $D$ is diagonal residual variance. Two flavours:

  • Statistical. $B$ from PCA. Factors are eigendirections.
  • Fundamental. $B$ from observable factors (industry, style, country, currency). The BARRA approach.

Factor covariance is well-conditioned for any $T$ as long as $K \ll N$. Standard in equity risk models for $N > 100$.

4. Hierarchical / clustered covariance

Cluster assets by correlation distance. Build a block covariance: full sample covariance within block, shrunken (or factor-only) between blocks. Useful when there are obvious sectors or regions. Bridge to HRP (Lesson 15).

5. Picking the right estimator

Heuristic decision tree:

  • $N < 10$, $T > 100 N$: sample is fine.
  • $N$ moderate, $T \approx 30 N$: Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage.
  • $N$ large, $T < 5 N$: RMT cleaning or factor model.
  • Time-varying environment: EWMA.
  • Equity universes with clear sectors: factor model.
  • Heterogeneous universes (cross-asset): hierarchical block.
In practice (Macrosynergy art 37, 99, 89): Macrosynergy uses PCA (article 37) to extract macro factors and reduce dimensionality. Article 99 surveys six volatility estimators (the diagonal of $\Sigma$). Article 89 uses the eigenstructure of correlation matrices to define market regimes. They almost never use raw sample covariance; the factor model approach dominates because it forces interpretable structure.

5b. Deep dive: RMT eigenvalue cleaning step by step

RMT cleaning (Laloux et al., 2000) is conceptually elegant: any eigenvalue inside the Marcenko-Pastur "noise interval" is treated as noise. Replace it with the average. Anything outside is "signal" and kept. Let me walk through what this does to a real-looking spectrum.

The procedure

  1. Compute sample covariance $S$.
  2. Eigendecompose: $S = V \Lambda V^\top$.
  3. Determine the noise interval $[\lambda_-, \lambda_+]$ where $\lambda_\pm = \bar \sigma^2 (1 \pm \sqrt{N/T})^2$, with $\bar \sigma^2$ the mean eigenvalue.
  4. For each eigenvalue: if outside $\lambda_+$, keep it. If inside $[\lambda_-, \lambda_+]$, replace with the average $\bar \lambda_{\text{noise}}$.
  5. Reconstruct $\hat S = V \hat \Lambda V^\top$.

Numerical example

$N = 50$ assets, $T = 100$ months. Suppose the true covariance has 3 dominant factors (market, size, value) with variance contributions 0.40, 0.08, 0.04, and 47 idiosyncratic dimensions each with variance 0.01.

The MP interval for $T/N = 2$ is $[\lambda_-, \lambda_+] \approx [0.086, 2.91]$ in units where $\bar \sigma^2 = 1$. Scaled to actual variance, this is roughly $[0.001, 0.029]$.

Sample eigenvalues (sorted descending) might look like:

  • $\lambda_1 = 0.42$ (market signal, well above $\lambda_+$, keep)
  • $\lambda_2 = 0.085$ (size signal, above $\lambda_+ \approx 0.029$, keep)
  • $\lambda_3 = 0.045$ (value signal, above $\lambda_+$, keep)
  • $\lambda_4$ to $\lambda_{50}$: range from 0.001 to 0.028 (all in the noise band)

Replace eigenvalues 4 to 50 with their average. The first three signals are preserved; the residual cloud is collapsed to its mean. The trace is preserved (total variance unchanged) but the condition number drops from ~400 to ~50.

Why nonlinear shrinkage often beats RMT

RMT cleaning is binary: either the eigenvalue is "signal" or "noise". Nonlinear shrinkage (Ledoit-Wolf 2017) applies a continuous shrinkage profile based on the precise position in the eigenvalue distribution. The largest eigenvalues get nearly their sample value; the smallest get pulled toward the average. Smoother and typically better OOS, especially when signal-noise boundary is fuzzy.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Bias-variance for covariance estimators

Sample $S$ has zero bias and high variance. Shrinkage trades bias for variance. Factor models have parametric bias from the factor structure assumption but zero variance for any $T$ (given known $B$). RMT cleaning is asymptotically unbiased and lower variance than sample.

6.2 Marcenko-Pastur for cleaning

For $T$ Gaussian observations on $N$ assets with true $\Sigma = \sigma^2 I$, sample eigenvalues lie in $[\sigma^2 (1 - \sqrt{N/T})^2, \sigma^2(1 + \sqrt{N/T})^2]$ asymptotically. Eigenvalues outside this interval reflect real signal.

6.3 Single-factor model derivation

Assume $r_i = \alpha_i + \beta_i r_m + \epsilon_i$ with $\epsilon_i$ idiosyncratic. Then $\Sigma_{ij} = \beta_i \beta_j \sigma_m^2 + \delta_{ij} \sigma_{\epsilon_i}^2$. Estimate $\beta$ from $T$ regressions. Total parameters: $2N + 1$ vs $N(N+1)/2$ for sample. Stable for any $T > 30$.

6.4 Nonlinear shrinkage (Ledoit-Wolf 2017)

Apply different shrinkage intensity to different eigenvalues, calibrated via the Stieltjes transform of the limiting eigenvalue distribution. Optimal in MSE among rotation-equivariant estimators.

6.5 EWMA stationarity

EWMA is non-stationary in general, but its conditional variance equation $\sigma_t^2 = \alpha r_{t-1}^2 + (1 - \alpha) \sigma_{t-1}^2$ is GARCH(1,1) with no constant term. The unconditional variance is undefined unless you add a constant.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: All six estimators

from sklearn.covariance import LedoitWolf, OAS

def all_estimators(returns):
    S = np.cov(returns.T)
    ewma = returns.ewm(halflife=22).cov().iloc[-len(returns.columns):]
    lw = LedoitWolf().fit(returns).covariance_
    # single factor
    market = returns.mean(axis=1)
    betas = np.array([np.cov(returns[c], market)[0,1] / market.var() for c in returns.columns])
    sf = np.outer(betas, betas) * market.var() + np.diag(np.diag(S - np.outer(betas, betas) * market.var()))
    return {'sample': S, 'ewma': ewma, 'lw': lw, 'sf': sf}

Exercise B: RMT cleaning from scratch

def rmt_clean(S, T):
    N = S.shape[0]
    eigvals, eigvecs = np.linalg.eigh(S)
    sigma2 = eigvals.mean()
    lambda_plus = sigma2 * (1 + np.sqrt(N / T)) ** 2
    noise_mask = eigvals < lambda_plus
    eigvals[noise_mask] = eigvals[noise_mask].mean()
    return eigvecs @ np.diag(eigvals) @ eigvecs.T
