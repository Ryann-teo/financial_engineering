0. Reading the controls

Kelly fraction $f$

The fraction of full Kelly. $f = 1$ is full Kelly. $f = 0.25$ is "quarter Kelly" (popular in practice). $f > 1$ is over-Kelly (catastrophic).

True Sharpe

The "ground truth" reward-to-risk. In real life unknown. Higher = more aggressive Kelly.

Number of paths

Monte Carlo simulation count. Each path is one possible realisation of $T$ years of returns.

1. Single-bet Kelly

The original problem (Kelly 1956): you face a sequence of independent bets, each with probability $p$ of paying $b$ to 1. What fraction of your wealth should you bet to maximise long-run growth?

$$f^\star = \frac{bp - q}{b}, \quad q = 1 - p$$

Kelly's derivation: maximise $\mathbb{E}[\log W]$ where $W$ is terminal wealth. The fraction $f^\star$ is the unique solution that gives the highest geometric growth rate. Bet less than $f^\star$ and you give up growth. Bet more than $f^\star$ and your growth rate falls (and at $f \ge 2 f^\star$ you eventually go bankrupt with probability 1).

2. Multi-asset Kelly

For continuous-time portfolio choice with log utility, Kelly is

$$w^\star = \Sigma^{-1} (\mu - r_f \mathbf{1})$$

This is exactly the tangency portfolio (Lesson 5) divided by 1. Or equivalently: Markowitz with $\lambda = 1$. So Kelly is what you get when you optimise mean-variance utility with risk aversion exactly equal to log utility's effective risk aversion.

The Kelly fraction matters more than the Kelly direction. Most practitioners agree on the direction (the Markowitz solution); they disagree on the magnitude.

3. Why nobody runs full Kelly

Full Kelly maximises long-run growth in expectation. But:

  • Drawdowns are extreme. Full Kelly has 50% drawdown probability of about 50% (rough rule). 80% drawdown probability is non-negligible.
  • Estimation error compounds. If your estimate $\hat \mu$ is biased up by 50%, full Kelly is bet at $1.5 f^\star$, which is on the wrong side of the growth-vs-bankruptcy tradeoff.
  • Career risk. Real investors face redemption risk. A 50% drawdown causes capital flight, killing the strategy before it can compound back.
  • The variance of log wealth is huge. Even at full Kelly, terminal wealth has high variance.

4. Fractional Kelly

$w = f \cdot w^{\text{Kelly}}$ for some $f \in (0, 1)$. Common choices:

  • $f = 0.25$ (quarter Kelly). Common among professional gamblers and some hedge funds. Gives up roughly 25% of growth rate but cuts variance by a factor of 4.
  • $f = 0.5$ (half Kelly). Gives up 75% of growth rate (note: not 50%). Variance cut by 4x.
  • $f = 0.10$ to $0.20$. Conservative. Used by pension funds and risk-averse institutions.

The math: at fraction $f$, the expected log growth is $f \cdot g - (1-f)^2 \cdot 0$ in expectation but variance is reduced. Calculus says the maximum is at $f = 1$ but the variance-adjusted growth (Sharpe-of-log-wealth) peaks earlier.

5. Connection to Merton

Lesson 19 derived Merton's solution: $w^\star = (\mu - r_f) / (\gamma \sigma^2)$. For log utility ($\gamma = 1$), this is exactly Kelly. For $\gamma = 4$ (typical CRRA), it's quarter-Kelly. The risk aversion parameter and the Kelly fraction are the same dial under different names.

So fractional Kelly with $f = 1/\gamma$ matches CRRA utility with risk aversion $\gamma$. Most institutional investors implicitly target $f \approx 0.2$ to $0.5$, equivalent to $\gamma$ in the 2 to 5 range.

In practice (Macrosynergy / AQR): Macrosynergy doesn't use Kelly directly. Their vol targeting (Lesson 21) is implicitly fractional Kelly: target a fixed vol level rather than maximise log growth. Empirically, MS strategies sit in the $f \approx 0.3$ to $0.5$ range when expressed in Kelly units. AQR's risk parity products are also implicit fractional Kelly. Full Kelly is the academic benchmark; fractional Kelly is what survives in production.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Kelly's growth-rate derivation

For a single bet with prob $p$ to win $b$ and prob $q = 1-p$ to lose 1, the log growth at fraction $f$ is

$$g(f) = p \log(1 + bf) + q \log(1 - f)$$

Maximise: $g'(f) = bp/(1 + bf) - q/(1 - f) = 0$. Solving: $f^\star = (bp - q) / b$.

6.2 Multi-asset log utility

Wealth dynamics: $dW = W [r_f + w^\top (\mu - r_f \mathbf{1})] dt + W w^\top \sigma dB$. Apply Ito to $\log W$:

$$d\log W = [r_f + w^\top (\mu - r_f \mathbf{1}) - \tfrac{1}{2} w^\top \Sigma w] dt + w^\top \sigma dB$$

Take expectation, maximise over $w$: first-order condition $\mu - r_f \mathbf{1} = \Sigma w$, so $w^\star = \Sigma^{-1} (\mu - r_f \mathbf{1})$.

6.3 The half-Kelly variance reduction

Variance of log wealth scales as $f^2 \cdot \sigma_{\text{port}}^2$. Half Kelly has variance one-quarter of full Kelly. Mean log growth at $f = 0.5$ is $0.5 g_{\max} - (0.5)^2 g_{\max} / 1 = 0.25 g_{\max}$. Wait that's wrong; the curvature gives $g(f) = f g_{\max}(1) - 0.5 (f - 1)^2 g_{\max}(2)$. The exact formula depends on Sharpe, but at $f = 0.5$ you keep about 75% of growth at 25% of variance.

6.4 Probability of drawdown

For a Brownian motion log-wealth process with drift $g$ and volatility $\sigma$, the probability of ever falling to fraction $\alpha$ of starting wealth is $\alpha^{2g/\sigma^2}$. For full Kelly, $g = \sigma^2/2$, giving $P(\text{50\% DD}) = 0.5^1 = 50\%$.

6.5 Kelly under estimation error

If you estimate $\hat \mu$ with bias and run full Kelly on it, your effective fraction is $\hat \mu / \mu_{\text{true}}$. A 30% upward bias on $\hat \mu$ produces $f \approx 1.3$ Kelly, which is in the danger zone. This is the practical reason for fractional Kelly: a margin of safety against estimation error.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: Single-bet Kelly

def kelly_fraction(win_prob, payoff_ratio):
    return (payoff_ratio * win_prob - (1 - win_prob)) / payoff_ratio

Exercise B: Multi-asset Kelly

def kelly_weights(mu, Sigma, rf=0):
    return np.linalg.solve(Sigma, mu - rf)

Exercise C: Monte Carlo Kelly simulation

def kelly_simulate(mu, sigma, fraction, T=20, n_paths=10000, dt=1/252, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    f = fraction
    n = int(T / dt)
    g_dt = (f * mu - 0.5 * f**2 * sigma**2) * dt
    s_dt = f * sigma * np.sqrt(dt)
    log_W = g_dt * np.arange(n) + s_dt * rng.standard_normal((n_paths, n)).cumsum(axis=1)
    return np.exp(log_W)

Question 1, conceptual

Question 2, coding

Question 3, numerical

For Sharpe = 1.0, full Kelly's expected log growth is 0.5. Half Kelly's expected log growth is approximately 0.375. What is the percent of growth retained?

Answer: 75%.
