0. Reading the controls

Number of signals

2 to 6 signals stacked. Macrosynergy strategies often use 4 to 8 macro themes per asset.

Average signal IC

The mean information coefficient across signals. Macro signals typically run 0.04 to 0.12.

Signal correlation

Average pairwise correlation among signals. High correlation hurts diversification benefits.

1. The blending problem

You have $K$ signals $s_1, \ldots, s_K$, each predicting future returns with its own IC. Build a composite $s = \sum_k w_k s_k$ that maximises predictive power.

Three approaches in increasing sophistication:

  1. Equal weight: $w_k = 1/K$.
  2. IC weight: $w_k \propto \text{IC}_k$.
  3. Optimal weight (Grinold-Kahn): $w \propto \Sigma_s^{-1} \text{IC}$, where $\Sigma_s$ is signal covariance.

2. Equal-weight blending

The Macrosynergy default. Take all signals, z-score within date, average. No optimisation.

Pros: zero overfitting risk. Robust to estimation error in IC. Easy to explain.

Cons: gives identical weight to a great signal and a marginal one. Misses correlation.

Empirically, equal weight is hard to beat by much because the upside of "smart" weighting is small and the downside (estimation error) is large.

3. IC-weighted blending

Set $w_k$ proportional to historical IC. Higher-IC signals get more weight.

Issues:

  • Historical IC is a noisy estimate of future IC.
  • Selection bias: signals with high in-sample IC are partly lucky.
  • If you also chose the signal set based on IC, double-counting.

In practice, IC-weighting helps modestly when the IC differences are large and statistically significant. For most macro signal sets, equal weight is within 5% of IC-weighted out of sample.

4. Orthogonalisation

Two correlated signals carry overlapping information. Combining them naively double-counts.

Gram-Schmidt: project signal 2 onto the space orthogonal to signal 1.

$$s_2^{\text{orth}} = s_2 - \frac{\langle s_1, s_2 \rangle}{\langle s_1, s_1 \rangle} s_1$$

The orthogonalised signal carries only the information not in signal 1. Combining signal 1 + orthogonalised signal 2 has no double counting.

Repeat for $s_3$ orthogonal to span of $\{s_1, s_2\}$, etc. Result: an orthonormal basis for the signal space.

5. Grinold's law and breadth

The fundamental law of active management:

$$\text{IR} \approx \text{IC} \cdot \sqrt{\text{breadth}}$$

where breadth is the number of independent bets per year. For $K$ signals on $N$ assets at frequency $f$ per year, breadth is at most $K \cdot N \cdot f$.

Critical: independent bets. Correlated signals reduce effective breadth. The "diversified breadth" rather than the "nominal breadth".

5b. Deep dive: orthogonalisation in practice

Two correlated signals carry overlapping information. Orthogonalisation extracts the unique part of each. Used right, it can double a portfolio's effective breadth. Used wrong, it amplifies noise.

Worked example: trend and value on FX

Suppose you have two FX signals on G10 currencies: 12-month trend and 5-year mean reversion (value). Empirically these are negatively correlated (~ -0.20): when a currency has been trending up, it tends to be far from its long-term value, and vice versa.

Naive equal-weight composite: gives both signals equal weight. Effective IC depends on the correlation: with $\rho = -0.20$, $K = 2$, individual IC = 0.06 each, the composite IC is 0.06 * sqrt(2) / sqrt(1 + (-0.2)) = 0.095. Slightly better than $\sqrt{2}$ because the negative correlation helps.

Orthogonalised composite: project value onto the space orthogonal to trend. The orthogonalised value carries information independent of trend's information. Then weight trend and orthogonalised-value equally. The effective IC becomes 0.06 * sqrt(2) = 0.085 if we naively assume independence after orthogonalisation.

Wait, that's lower. The benefit of orthogonalisation comes from correctness, not from raw IC. Orthogonalisation prevents you from double-weighting overlapping information.

When orthogonalisation helps

  • High correlation among signals. If 5 signals all correlate at 0.8, equal weighting essentially gives you 1 signal. Orthogonalising recovers the true 5-dimensional information set.
  • Stacking related signals. Multiple value variants (P/B, P/E, P/S, P/CF). Each adds incremental info; orthogonalising prevents double counting.

When orthogonalisation hurts

  • Already independent signals. Negative or zero correlation. Orthogonalisation is a no-op or counterproductive (numerical noise).
  • Sign sensitivity. The orthogonal component of value (after subtracting trend) might have opposite sign to value itself when trend is dominant. Counterintuitive, sometimes wrong.
  • Estimation noise on the orthogonalisation. Computing the projection requires estimating signal correlation, which is itself noisy.

The Macrosynergy approach (conceptual orthogonalisation)

Rather than statistical orthogonalisation, MS uses theoretical orthogonalisation. They design signals from the start to be theoretically independent. For example: trend (price-based) and macro growth (fundamental). By construction these are independent at the conceptual level. Statistical orthogonalisation is unnecessary.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Composite IC for $K$ correlated signals

For equal-weight composite of $K$ signals each with IC $\rho_k$ and pairwise correlation $\rho$ (assume identical for simplicity):

$$\text{IC}_{\text{composite}} = \frac{\bar \rho \sqrt{K}}{\sqrt{1 + (K - 1) \rho}}$$

For $\rho = 0$: $\text{IC}_{\text{composite}} = \bar \rho \sqrt{K}$. For $\rho = 1$: $\text{IC}_{\text{composite}} = \bar \rho$. Composite IC scales with sqrt(K) only when signals are uncorrelated.

6.2 Optimal blend weights (Grinold-Kahn)

$$w^\star = \Sigma_s^{-1} \mathbf{IC}$$

where $\Sigma_s$ is the signal covariance and $\mathbf{IC}$ is the IC vector. Closed form. In practice estimation noise in $\Sigma_s$ ruins this; equal weight wins out-of-sample.

6.3 Effective N from signal correlation matrix

The eigenvalue spectrum of the signal correlation matrix tells you effective dimension. If $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_K$ are eigenvalues:

$$N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{(\sum \lambda_k)^2}{\sum \lambda_k^2}$$

Identity matrix: $N_{\text{eff}} = K$. Single dominant factor: $N_{\text{eff}} \to 1$.

6.4 The transfer coefficient

Signal IC translates to portfolio alpha through the transfer coefficient TC, which captures how cleanly the alpha translates given constraints (Lesson 25).

$$\text{IR} = \text{IC} \cdot \sqrt{\text{breadth}} \cdot \text{TC}$$

Long-only TC ~0.5; market-neutral hedge fund TC ~0.85. Constraints cost.

6.5 Time-varying signal weights

Beta learning (art 49, Lesson 10): allow $w_k(t)$ to vary slowly. Use rolling regression of forward returns on signals. Smooth via shrinkage. Modest gains over static equal weight.

Exercise C: Orthogonalisation

def gram_schmidt(signals):
    out = [signals[:, 0]]
    for k in range(1, signals.shape[1]):
        s = signals[:, k]
        for prev in out:
            s = s - (prev @ s / (prev @ prev)) * prev
        out.append(s)
    return np.array(out).T
