0. Reading the controls

Vol heterogeneity

Slide between assets with similar volatilities and assets with very different volatilities. ERC weights respond inversely to vol, but adjust for correlations.

Correlation

The cross-asset correlation. ERC behaves differently in low vs high correlation regimes.

Macro tilt strength

Lets you bias the risk budget toward "growth" or "defensive" assets based on a macro signal.

1. From risk budgeting to ERC

In Lesson 7 we learned the Euler decomposition: $\sigma_p = \sum_i \text{RC}_i$. ERC is the special case where all $\text{RC}_i$ are equal:

$$\frac{w_i (\Sigma w)_i}{\sigma_p} = \frac{1}{N} \sigma_p \quad \forall i$$

Equivalently: $w_i (\Sigma w)_i = w_j (\Sigma w)_j$ for all $i, j$. This is a system of $N$ nonlinear equations.

2. The Spinu convex form

Spinu (2013) proved the equivalent convex problem:

$$\min_{w > 0} \tfrac{1}{2} w^\top \Sigma w - \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \log w_i$$

The log barrier ensures positive weights. The first-order conditions match the ERC equations exactly. Standard convex solvers (cvxpy with `cp.log`) handle this in 5 lines.

Maillard, Roncalli, and Teiletche (2010) proved existence and uniqueness under positivity. Each ERC problem has exactly one solution.

3. Cyclical coordinate descent

Faster than convex solvers for ERC: at each step, fix all $w_j$ except $w_i$, solve a 1D quadratic for $w_i$. The 1D update has closed form. Iterate until $\|w - w_{\text{old}}\| < \text{tol}$. Converges in 10 to 50 iterations.

def erc_ccd(Sigma, max_iter=1000, tol=1e-9):
    n = Sigma.shape[0]
    w = np.ones(n) / n
    for _ in range(max_iter):
        w_old = w.copy()
        for i in range(n):
            num = (Sigma @ w)[i] - Sigma[i, i] * w[i]
            disc = num ** 2 + 4 * Sigma[i, i] * (1 / n) * (w @ Sigma @ w)
            w[i] = (-num + np.sqrt(disc)) / (2 * Sigma[i, i])
        w = w / w.sum()
        if np.linalg.norm(w - w_old) < tol: break
    return w

4. Risk parity in practice

Risk parity is a portfolio strategy built on ERC across asset classes. The classic implementation: 25% equity risk, 25% bond risk, 25% commodity risk, 25% gold risk. To match equity-only Sharpe, leverage 1.5x to 2x is needed because the unlevered portfolio is too low-risk.

The leverage problem. Borrowing costs above the risk-free rate eat into the Sharpe gain. Asness, Frazzini, Pedersen (2012) show risk parity still beats 60/40 after realistic borrowing costs. The 2022 inflation shock hurt risk parity disproportionately because both bonds and equities sold off.

5. Macro-aware risk parity

Plain ERC ignores expected returns and macro context. Macrosynergy art 23 and 88 propose "macro-aware risk parity": tilt the risk budget by macro signals. Examples:

  • If the macro signal favours growth assets (low real rates, expanding PMI), tilt risk toward equities.
  • If inflation accelerating, tilt risk toward commodities and away from nominal bonds.

The implementation is a risk-budgeting problem (Lesson 7) with non-uniform $b$ derived from macro factors. Sharpe improves from ~0.8 (plain ERC) to ~1.3 in the cross-asset case (art 23).

In practice (Macrosynergy art 23, 88, 285): Macrosynergy uses ERC as the baseline and adds macro factor tilts as the alpha. Article 285 applies this to cross-country bond allocation. The structure is "two-fund": fund A is the risk-balanced book (ERC), fund B is the macro overlay. This is a clean separation between risk allocation and return prediction. Most MS systematic products use this structure.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Why Spinu's log barrier works

The first-order condition $\partial \mathcal{L} / \partial w_i = (\Sigma w)_i - 1 / (N w_i) = 0$ rearranges to $w_i (\Sigma w)_i = 1/N$. Sum over $i$: total risk equals $\sum_i 1/N = 1$, scaled by $w^\top \Sigma w = $ portfolio variance. So all asset risks equal $1/N$ of total. Multiply through by $w^\top \Sigma w$ and you get the ERC condition.

6.2 Existence and uniqueness

The Spinu objective is convex. The log barrier forces $w_i > 0$. The objective is coercive (goes to infinity at the boundary). Therefore a unique global minimum exists.

6.3 Risk parity Sharpe vs 60/40 Sharpe

If all assets had the same Sharpe and were uncorrelated, ERC = inverse-vol weight, and the portfolio Sharpe equals individual asset Sharpe. With correlations, ERC Sharpe is bounded above by the most-correlated-asset Sharpe.

6.4 The leverage cost arithmetic

Levered Sharpe = unlevered Sharpe minus (financing rate above risk-free) divided by vol. For 1.5x leverage at 1% above risk-free: cost = 0.5% / vol. At 10% vol, cost = 5 bp Sharpe. Modest.

6.5 Macro tilt as a risk budget perturbation

If $b_i^{\text{macro}}$ is the tilted budget, solve $w_i (\Sigma w)_i = b_i^{\text{macro}} (w^\top \Sigma w)$. The Spinu objective generalises to $\tfrac{1}{2} w^\top \Sigma w - \sum_i b_i^{\text{macro}} \log w_i$. Same algorithm with $1/N$ replaced by $b_i^{\text{macro}}$.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: ERC with cvxpy

import cvxpy as cp
def erc_cvxpy(Sigma, b=None):
    n = Sigma.shape[0]
    if b is None: b = np.ones(n) / n
    w = cp.Variable(n, pos=True)
    obj = cp.Minimize(0.5 * cp.quad_form(w, Sigma) - b @ cp.log(w))
    cp.Problem(obj).solve()
    return w.value / w.value.sum()
