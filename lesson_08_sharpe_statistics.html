0. Reading the controls

True Sharpe ratio

The "ground truth" Sharpe of the underlying strategy. In real life you never know this; the whole point of statistics is to estimate it from data. In the demos here we set it explicitly so we can see the gap between truth and observation.

Sample size $T$

Number of monthly observations used to estimate the Sharpe. 60 = 5 years. 120 = 10 years. 240 = 20 years. The standard error of an estimated Sharpe scales roughly as $1/\sqrt{T}$.

Number of trials $N$

How many strategies were tested in the search process. If you grid-search 10 hyperparameters with 5 values each, $N = 5^{10} \approx 10^7$. The Deflated Sharpe Ratio penalises by $\log N$.

Skew and excess kurtosis

Higher moments of the return distribution. Affect the variance of the Sharpe estimator. Negative skew or high kurtosis inflates the standard error of $\hat{SR}$.

1. The Sharpe ratio

The standard reward-risk metric:

$$\text{SR} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[r] - r_f}{\sigma(r)}$$

Annualised. Period choice matters: a daily Sharpe of 0.06 annualises to about $0.06 \cdot \sqrt{252} \approx 0.95$ under independence.

Three things to remember about Sharpe.

1. It is a population parameter. The true mean and true volatility are unknown. The reported number is always an estimate.

2. It assumes finite variance and stationarity. If returns have infinite variance (Cauchy-like), Sharpe is meaningless. If the regime shifts, the Sharpe of one regime tells you little about the next.

3. The square-root-of-time annualisation requires independence. When returns are autocorrelated, the annualised Sharpe is biased. Lo (2002) gives the autocorrelation-adjusted formula.

2. The sampling distribution of estimated Sharpe

For IID Gaussian returns, Lo (2002) shows that the estimated Sharpe satisfies asymptotically:

$$\hat{\text{SR}} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\text{SR}, \frac{1 + \text{SR}^2 / 2}{T}\right)$$

So the standard error is

$$\text{SE}(\hat{\text{SR}}) \approx \sqrt{\frac{1 + \text{SR}^2 / 2}{T}}$$

For $T = 60$ months and true SR = 1.0, the SE is $\sqrt{(1 + 0.5) / 60} \approx 0.16$. A 95% confidence interval is roughly $\hat{\text{SR}} \pm 0.31$. So a reported Sharpe of 1.0 from 5 years of monthly data could correspond to a true Sharpe anywhere from 0.7 to 1.3.

For non-Gaussian returns, Mertens (2002) gives the more general formula incorporating skew and kurtosis. The variance of $\hat{\text{SR}}$ is inflated by negative skew and excess kurtosis.

3. The Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio

Bailey and Lopez de Prado (2012) define

$$\text{PSR}(\text{SR}^*) = \Phi\left(\frac{(\hat{\text{SR}} - \text{SR}^*) \sqrt{T - 1}}{\sqrt{1 - \hat{\gamma}_3 \hat{\text{SR}} + \tfrac{\hat{\gamma}_4 - 1}{4} \hat{\text{SR}}^2}}\right)$$

where $\hat{\gamma}_3$ is sample skew, $\hat{\gamma}_4$ is sample kurtosis (not excess), and $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. The PSR is the probability that the true Sharpe exceeds a benchmark $\text{SR}^*$, given your sample. PSR > 0.95 is the usual "real strategy" bar.

4. The Deflated Sharpe Ratio

If you tried $N$ strategies and reported the best one, your "best" Sharpe is biased upward by selection. The DSR adjusts:

$$\text{DSR} = \text{PSR}\left(\mathbb{E}[\max_n \hat{\text{SR}}_n \mid \text{SR}_n = 0]\right)$$

The benchmark $\text{SR}^*$ is now the expected maximum across $N$ trials under the null hypothesis that all $N$ strategies have zero true Sharpe. From extreme value theory:

$$\mathbb{E}[\max_n \hat{\text{SR}}_n \mid \text{null}] \approx \frac{(1 - \gamma) \Phi^{-1}(1 - 1/N) + \gamma \Phi^{-1}(1 - 1/(N e))}{\sqrt{T}}$$

where $\gamma \approx 0.5772$ is the Euler-Mascheroni constant. This rises like $\sqrt{2 \log N / T}$, the classical extreme-value scaling.

Slide $N$ from 1 to 10000. The expected max Sharpe under the null rises with $\sqrt{\log N}$. By $N = 1000$, even a true-Sharpe-zero strategy will look like it has Sharpe 0.5 to 0.8 in the best of 1000 trials. The DSR is brutal but honest.

5. Sortino, Calmar, Information Ratio

Sortino ratio: replace volatility with downside deviation. $\text{Sortino} = (\bar r - r_f) / \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[\min(r, 0)^2]}$. Useful when the return distribution is asymmetric (negative skew).

Calmar ratio: annualised return divided by max drawdown. Used in CTAs and managed futures, where path-dependent risk matters more than vol.

Information ratio: like Sharpe but applied to active returns vs a benchmark. $\text{IR} = (\bar r - \bar r_b) / \sigma(r - r_b)$. Used for benchmark-relative mandates.

In practice (Macrosynergy art 50, 65, 204): The MS protocol for evaluating a signal does NOT lead with Sharpe. It leads with the predictive correlation (signal vs forward return), then accuracy/hit rate, then naive PnL ratio. Sharpe is reported but never trusted as a single number. Article 204 ("Systematic trading strategies fooled by live records") quantifies how much in-sample Sharpe overstates live Sharpe across the MS catalog: a 30 to 50% degradation is typical. That gap is exactly what DSR is designed to predict and reject.

Read: a strategy with true Sharpe 1.0 over 5 years has 95% CI of about $1.0 \pm 0.32$. Anything between 0.7 and 1.3 is consistent with "true Sharpe is 1". So if you see 0.8 in 5 years and someone else sees 1.1 in 5 years, statistically you have the same strategy.

Years of data needed to distinguish two Sharpes

Suppose strategy A has true Sharpe 0.8 and strategy B has true Sharpe 0.5. How many years of monthly returns do you need to confidently say A beats B?

The required $T$ for 95% confidence is approximately $T \approx 2 \cdot (1.96 \cdot \sqrt{1 + 0.5(SR_A^2 + SR_B^2)} / (SR_A - SR_B))^2$.

Plug in: $T \approx 2 \cdot (1.96 \cdot \sqrt{1 + 0.5 \cdot (0.64 + 0.25)} / 0.3)^2 = 2 \cdot (1.96 \cdot 1.20 / 0.3)^2 \approx 123$ months $\approx$ 10 years.

You need 10 years of monthly data to distinguish a Sharpe-0.8 from a Sharpe-0.5 strategy. This is why "track record" arguments rarely settle anything.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Why the standard error of Sharpe scales as $1/\sqrt{T}$

Both $\hat \mu$ and $\hat \sigma$ are sample moments with standard errors $\sigma/\sqrt{T}$ and $\sigma/\sqrt{2T}$ respectively. The Sharpe is the ratio. By the delta method, $\text{Var}(\hat{\text{SR}}) = (1 + \text{SR}^2 / 2) / T$ for IID Gaussian returns. The "1" term comes from the mean estimation error; the "$\text{SR}^2 / 2$" term comes from the volatility estimation error.

6.2 Mertens (2002) for non-Gaussian returns

$\text{Var}(\hat{\text{SR}}) = (1 - \gamma_3 \text{SR} + \tfrac{\gamma_4 - 1}{4} \text{SR}^2) / T$ where $\gamma_3$ is skew and $\gamma_4$ is kurtosis (not excess). Negative skew adds variance because $\gamma_3 \text{SR} < 0$, so the term $-\gamma_3 \text{SR} > 0$. High kurtosis adds variance through the $\text{SR}^2$ coefficient.

6.3 The expected max Sharpe under the null

For $N$ IID standard normal random variables, the expected maximum is approximately $\Phi^{-1}(1 - 1/N) + \gamma \cdot \phi(\Phi^{-1}(1 - 1/N))^{-1}$. For large $N$, this grows as $\sqrt{2 \log N}$. Translated to Sharpe space (divide by $\sqrt{T}$), the bias grows as $\sqrt{2 \log N / T}$.

6.4 Why the DSR uses PSR not raw normal

The DSR is the PSR evaluated at the bias-corrected benchmark. This is more robust than naive z-scoring because PSR accounts for higher moments of the return distribution.

6.5 Annualisation under autocorrelation

For a return process with first-order autocorrelation $\rho$, the variance of the $T$-period sum is

$$\text{Var}(\sum_{t=1}^T r_t) = T \sigma^2 \cdot q(T, \rho), \quad q(T, \rho) = 1 + 2 \sum_{k=1}^{T-1} (1 - k/T) \rho^k$$

So the annualised Sharpe is $\hat{\text{SR}}_{\text{annual}} = \hat{\text{SR}}_{\text{period}} \cdot \sqrt{T / q(T, \rho)}$. For $\rho = 0.2$ and monthly data, $q \approx 1.5$. The naive annualisation overstates the Sharpe by roughly 25%. This is why bond Sharpes look better than they are.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: Sharpe with confidence interval

import numpy as np
def sharpe_with_ci(returns, T_per_year=12):
    mu = returns.mean() * T_per_year
    sigma = returns.std(ddof=1) * np.sqrt(T_per_year)
    sr = mu / sigma
    se = np.sqrt((1 + 0.5 * sr**2) / len(returns))
    return {'sr': sr, 'se': se, 'ci_low': sr - 1.96 * se, 'ci_high': sr + 1.96 * se}

Exercise B: Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio

from scipy.stats import norm, skew, kurtosis

def psr(returns, sr_benchmark=0):
    sr = returns.mean() / returns.std(ddof=1)
    T = len(returns)
    s = skew(returns)
    k = kurtosis(returns, fisher=False)  # not excess
    z = (sr - sr_benchmark) * np.sqrt(T - 1) / np.sqrt(1 - s * sr + (k - 1) / 4 * sr**2)
    return float(norm.cdf(z))

Exercise C: Deflated Sharpe Ratio

def expected_max_sr(N, T):
    gamma = 0.5772
    z1 = norm.ppf(1 - 1/N)
    z2 = norm.ppf(1 - 1/(N * np.e))
    return ((1 - gamma) * z1 + gamma * z2) / np.sqrt(T)

def dsr(returns, N_trials):
    sr = returns.mean() / returns.std(ddof=1)
    benchmark = expected_max_sr(N_trials, len(returns))
    return psr(returns, sr_benchmark=benchmark)

Exercise E: Bootstrap confidence interval for Sharpe

def bootstrap_sr_ci(returns, n_boot=10000):
    n = len(returns)
    srs = np.array([
        np.random.choice(returns.values, size=n, replace=True).mean() /
        np.random.choice(returns.values, size=n, replace=True).std(ddof=1)
        for _ in range(n_boot)
    ])
    return np.percentile(srs, [2.5, 97.5])
