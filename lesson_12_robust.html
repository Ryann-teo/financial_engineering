0. Reading the controls

Uncertainty radius $\kappa$

How big the uncertainty set is. Larger $\kappa$ means more conservative. $\kappa = 0$ recovers nominal Markowitz; $\kappa \to \infty$ approaches equal weight.

Geometry: box vs ellipsoidal

Box uncertainty: each $\mu_i$ varies independently in an interval. Ellipsoidal: joint variation, can capture correlation in estimation errors.

Method toggle

Robust SOCP, resampled efficiency, plain MV. Compare side-by-side.

1. The robust philosophy

Bayesian: average over uncertainty using a prior. Robust: optimise the worst case over an uncertainty set.

$$\max_w \min_{\mu \in \mathcal{U}} \left[ w^\top \mu - (\lambda/2) w^\top \Sigma w \right] \text{ s.t. } \mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$$

Read: pick $w$ to maximise utility under the worst $\mu$ in your uncertainty set $\mathcal{U}$. Robust optimisation gives a deterministic guarantee: no matter which $\mu \in \mathcal{U}$ realises, your portfolio's utility is at least the optimal value.

2. Uncertainty sets

  • Box: $\mathcal{U} = \{\mu : |\mu_i - \hat \mu_i| \le \delta_i\}$. Each component varies independently. Most conservative because it does not use correlation structure.
  • Ellipsoidal: $\mathcal{U} = \{\mu : (\mu - \hat \mu)^\top \Theta^{-1} (\mu - \hat \mu) \le \kappa^2\}$ for some matrix $\Theta$. Most popular: tractable as SOCP, captures joint estimation error.
  • Polyhedral: $\mathcal{U} = \{\mu : A \mu \le b\}$. Linear inequalities. Tractable as LP.
  • Wasserstein ball: $\mathcal{U}$ is a set of probability distributions within Wasserstein distance $\rho$ of an empirical distribution. The most modern formulation.

3. The robust SOCP

For ellipsoidal uncertainty on $\mu$:

$$\max_w \, \hat \mu^\top w - \kappa \sqrt{w^\top \Theta w} - (\lambda/2) w^\top \Sigma w$$

The penalty term $-\kappa \sqrt{w^\top \Theta w}$ penalises portfolios with high estimation-error exposure. This is a second-order cone program (SOCP), tractable in cvxpy with one line of code.

4. Resampled efficiency (Michaud)

Michaud (1998) proposed a non-parametric robustification: bootstrap your input data many times, solve Markowitz on each bootstrap, average the resulting weights. The averaged weights are smoother than any single optimisation. Equivalent to Bayesian with an implicit empirical prior.

5. Distributionally robust optimisation

Modern frontier. Instead of an uncertainty set on $\mu$, work with a set of probability distributions on returns:

$$\min_w \max_{P \in \mathcal{P}} \mathbb{E}_P[L(w, r)]$$

where $\mathcal{P}$ is, e.g., a Wasserstein ball around the empirical distribution. DRO unifies robust optimisation, Bayesian, and regularisation. Computational frontier work.

In practice (Macrosynergy art 4, 45): Macrosynergy's regression-based ML pipeline uses ridge (a flavour of robust optimisation) with adaptive shrinkage. Article 45's "signal reliability adjustment" is Bayesian-flavoured robust shrinkage that scales positions by the inverse of recent forecast error. The MS philosophy: never trust the optimiser's confidence. Use methods that produce stable, conservative weights. Robust optimisation aligns with this perfectly.

5b. Deep dive: calibrating $\kappa$ in practice

The uncertainty radius $\kappa$ is the central tuning parameter of robust optimisation. Pick it from data, not from intuition.

Calibration approach 1: confidence interval on $\mu$

If your $\hat\mu$ has standard error $s$ per component, the 95% ellipsoidal uncertainty set has $\kappa = \chi^2_{N, 0.95}^{1/2}$ scaled by $s$. For $N = 5$ assets and 95% confidence: $\kappa = \sqrt{11.07} \approx 3.32$. For $N = 50$: $\kappa = \sqrt{67.5} \approx 8.2$.

Calibration approach 2: cross-validation

Sweep $\kappa$ from 0 to 5. For each, run an out-of-sample backtest. Pick the $\kappa$ that maximises out-of-sample Sharpe. Typical optimum: $\kappa \approx 1$ to 2. Higher $\kappa$ over-shrinks toward equal weight.

Calibration approach 3: implied from "willingness to bear estimation risk"

The robust SOCP has a Lagrangian interpretation. The penalty $\kappa \sqrt{w^\top \Theta w}$ is the cost of estimation risk. Setting $\kappa = $ Sharpe of the tangency portfolio's $\mu$-uncertainty makes the robust solution match Bayesian shrinkage with prior precision $1/\Theta$. Typical: $\kappa = 0.5$ to 1.5.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Robust MV reformulation as SOCP

The inner minimisation $\min_{\mu \in \mathcal{U}} w^\top \mu$ over the ellipsoid $\{(\mu - \hat\mu)^\top \Theta^{-1} (\mu - \hat\mu) \le \kappa^2\}$ has closed form $\hat\mu^\top w - \kappa \sqrt{w^\top \Theta w}$ by Cauchy-Schwarz. Substituting back gives the outer max as a deterministic SOCP.

6.2 Equivalence to Bayesian shrinkage

For Gaussian uncertainty, robust MV with $\Theta = (\hat \Sigma)$ gives identical weights to Bayesian MV with shrinkage prior. Robust + Gaussian $\equiv$ ridge.

6.3 Wasserstein DRO as regularisation

Mohajerin Esfahani and Kuhn (2018) show that Wasserstein DRO is equivalent to a regularised empirical risk minimisation. The Wasserstein radius plays the role of regularisation strength.

6.4 Resampled vs robust

Resampled (Michaud) is averaged over uncertainty (Bayesian flavour). Robust SOCP is worst case (frequentist guarantee). Empirically, resampled often outperforms robust because real estimation errors are not as bad as the worst case.

6.5 Conservatism and the price of robustness

The robust portfolio gives up Sharpe to gain stability. The trade-off is parameterised by $\kappa$. Modern papers calibrate $\kappa$ to match a desired level of out-of-sample performance.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: Robust MV with cvxpy

import cvxpy as cp

def robust_mv(mu_hat, Theta_chol, Sigma, lam, kappa):
    n = len(mu_hat)
    w = cp.Variable(n)
    obj = cp.Maximize(mu_hat @ w - kappa * cp.norm(Theta_chol.T @ w, 2) - 0.5 * lam * cp.quad_form(w, Sigma))
    cp.Problem(obj, [cp.sum(w) == 1]).solve()
    return w.value

Exercise B: Resampled efficiency

def resampled_efficiency(returns, lam, n_boot=500, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    weights = []
    for _ in range(n_boot):
        idx = rng.integers(0, len(returns), size=len(returns))
        sample = returns[idx]
        mu_b = sample.mean(axis=0)
        Sigma_b = np.cov(sample.T)
        w = markowitz(mu_b, Sigma_b, lam)
        weights.append(w)
    return np.mean(weights, axis=0)
