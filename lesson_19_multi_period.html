0. Reading the controls

Investment horizon $T$

Number of years over which utility is evaluated. Long horizons amplify intertemporal hedging effects.

Risk aversion $\gamma$ (CRRA)

Constant Relative Risk Aversion. $\gamma = 1$ is log utility (Kelly investor). $\gamma > 1$ is more risk averse. $\gamma < 1$ is less risk averse.

State variable persistence

How quickly does the investment opportunity (e.g. equity premium) revert? High persistence = stronger hedging demand.

1. Why single-period is not enough

Single-period MV assumes you trade once, then sit for $T$ periods. Real investors trade continuously, and the optimal $w$ depends on:

  1. The current investment opportunity ($\mu_t$, $\Sigma_t$).
  2. Future investment opportunities (will $\mu$ rise or fall?).
  3. Wealth dynamics (compounding, leverage).
  4. Transaction costs (Lesson 20).

Single-period MV captures only (1). Multi-period adds (2), (3), (4). The biggest practical effect is (2): if the equity premium is mean-reverting, today's high premium predicts tomorrow's low premium, and you should hold less equity than single-period MV suggests.

2. Merton's intertemporal portfolio choice

Merton (1969, 1971) solves the continuous-time portfolio problem with CRRA utility $u(W) = W^{1-\gamma} / (1 - \gamma)$ and one risky asset following geometric Brownian motion. The optimal weight on the risky asset is

$$w^\star = \frac{\mu - r_f}{\gamma \sigma^2}$$

For $\gamma = 1$ (log utility): $w^\star = (\mu - r_f) / \sigma^2$. This is the Kelly weight (Lesson 22) divided by 1. For $\gamma = 5$ (typical): $w^\star = (\mu - r_f) / (5 \sigma^2)$, which is fractional Kelly with fraction $1/5$.

Notice: the Merton solution is single-period under GBM with constant $\mu$ and $\sigma$. Multi-period magic happens only when investment opportunities change over time.

3. The HJB equation

The full multi-period problem with stochastic investment opportunities solves the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman PDE:

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \max_w \left[u(c) + \mathcal{L} V \right] = 0$$

where $V(W, x, t)$ is the value function, $x$ is a state variable (e.g. dividend yield, term spread), $c$ is consumption, and $\mathcal{L}$ is the infinitesimal generator. The Bellman optimality condition. Solving HJB analytically requires special structure (e.g. CRRA + linear-Gaussian dynamics give the affine value function of Liu 2007 and Wachter 2002).

4. Intertemporal hedging demand

The optimal weight in the HJB-solved problem decomposes:

$$w^\star = \underbrace{\frac{\mu - r_f}{\gamma \sigma^2}}_{\text{myopic}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{\gamma} \cdot \frac{\partial \log V / \partial x}{\sigma_W \sigma_x \rho_{Wx}}}_{\text{hedging demand}}$$

The first term is the single-period Merton solution. The second is the intertemporal hedging demand: a tilt toward (or away from) the asset based on whether it hedges changes in future investment opportunities.

Empirical example: equities have negative correlation with their own future expected returns (high premium today predicts low premium tomorrow, and vice versa). This is why Campbell and Viceira (2002) show that long-horizon investors should hold MORE equities than single-period MV suggests, even though equities are more volatile in the short run. The dividend yield as a state variable creates a hedging demand.

5. Connection to Kelly

For log utility ($\gamma = 1$), Merton's solution equals Kelly: $w^\star = (\mu - r_f) / \sigma^2$. Log utility is the unique CRRA utility for which myopic = dynamic, regardless of how investment opportunities evolve. Kelly investors are myopic by virtue of their utility function, not because they ignore the future.

6. Practical multi-period: rolling reoptimisation

For most production systems, the HJB solution is intractable (state space too large, distributions too non-standard). The standard workaround:

  1. Re-estimate $\mu_t$, $\Sigma_t$ each period using rolling windows.
  2. Solve the single-period MV problem with current estimates.
  3. Apply turnover penalties (Lesson 20) to discourage over-trading.
  4. Repeat each rebalancing period.

This "naive multi-period" misses intertemporal hedging but captures the time-varying opportunity set. For most practitioners it's good enough.

In practice (Macrosynergy, AQR): Most macro and systematic shops use rolling reoptimisation rather than HJB. Macrosynergy's pipeline (art 39) re-evaluates signals each rebalancing period without forecasting their persistence. AQR's research suggests that long-horizon investors gain only modest utility from full HJB versus naive rolling, given realistic estimates of return predictability. Article 186 explores reinforcement learning as an alternative to HJB for trading systems.

6b. Deep dive: when does dynamic actually beat myopic?

The Merton hedging demand sounds elegant in theory. In practice, it adds Sharpe only when very specific conditions hold. Knowing them tells you when to bother.

The three conditions for non-trivial hedging demand

  1. Investment opportunities are time-varying. If $\mu_t$ and $\Sigma_t$ are constant, Merton collapses to single-period MV regardless of horizon.
  2. The state variable is partially predictable. If you cannot forecast where opportunities are heading, you cannot hedge against them.
  3. Risk aversion $\gamma \ne 1$. Log utility ($\gamma = 1$) is myopic by construction. Non-log CRRA is required for hedging demand.

Why most production systems use rolling MV

The hedging demand Sharpe gain is small relative to the cost of getting it wrong. Misspecifying the state-variable dynamics produces a Merton solution that's biased in one direction and stays there. Rolling reoptimisation is robust to misspecification because it adapts each period.

Campbell and Viceira (2002) calibrated a careful long-horizon model with conservative assumptions and got Sharpe gains of 0.05 to 0.10 over 20-year horizons. Real production systems usually leave this on the table because the cost of model risk exceeds the alpha.

7. Mathematical companion

7.1 Merton's solution under CRRA + GBM

Wealth follows $dW = W [r_f + w (\mu - r_f)] dt + W w \sigma dB$. Maximise $\mathbb{E}[u(W_T)]$. Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman gives a Riccati equation that solves analytically. The optimal $w^\star = (\mu - r_f) / (\gamma \sigma^2)$ is constant in time, only depends on parameters.

7.2 Why log utility is myopic

$u(W) = \log W$ implies $u(W \cdot R) = \log W + \log R$. Future utility is additively separable in current and future log wealth. Today's decision affects only today's $\log R$, independently of future decisions. This is the formal reason Kelly is myopic.

7.3 Campbell-Viceira hedging demand

Under linear-Gaussian state dynamics for log dividend yield, the hedging demand has closed form via affine value function. Sign and magnitude depend on the correlation between equity returns and innovations to the state variable. Empirically the sign is negative (mean-reverting equity premium), making long-horizon investors more equity-hungry.

7.4 Discrete-time HJB approximation

Discretise the state space and solve via dynamic programming. For two state variables and $T = 50$ periods, computational complexity is $O(N_x N_y T)$. Tractable for small problems, intractable for production.

7.5 Reinforcement learning as continuous HJB

Modern RL (Q-learning, actor-critic) learns the value function from simulated trajectories without explicit HJB solution. Promising for problems where the dynamics are too complex for analytical methods.
