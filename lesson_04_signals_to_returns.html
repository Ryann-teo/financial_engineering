0. Reading the controls

Information coefficient (IC)

The cross-sectional correlation between your signal and the next-period return. Typical macro signal IC: 0.04 to 0.15. Anything above 0.10 is strong. Above 0.20 is suspicious.

Breadth $N$

The number of independent bets per period. For a single-asset trend strategy with monthly rebalances, breadth is roughly 12 per year. For a 50-asset cross-sectional strategy on weekly rebalances, it is closer to 50 * 50 = 2500 per year.

Signal half-life

How long the signal's predictive power decays. Shorter = higher turnover. Macro signals (carry, value) have half-lives of months to years. Trend signals: weeks. Microstructure signals: hours.

Cross-sectional vs time-series

Cross-sectional: rank assets within a date, go long top, short bottom. Time-series: each asset judged on its own time series, no cross-comparison. Different breadth profiles.

1. The expected-return decomposition

Ilmanen (2011) decomposes any asset's expected return into building blocks:

$$\mathbb{E}[r] = \text{cash rate} + \text{term/duration} + \text{credit} + \text{equity premium} + \text{style tilts}$$

Each block is a "risk premium" that an investor earns for bearing a particular risk. The portfolio construction problem is: which blocks do you want exposure to, and in what proportion?

For shorter horizons, the same decomposition can be expressed dynamically: $\mathbb{E}_t[r_{t+1}] = \mu_{\text{equilibrium}} + \mu_{\text{macro}} + \mu_{\text{style}}$. The conditional means come from signals, not from sample means.

2. The four style premia (AQR / Ilmanen)

AQR's "Style Premia" framework (Asness, Ilmanen, Israel, Moskowitz 2015) identifies four robust risk premia that show up across asset classes:

  • Carry. What the asset pays you for holding it (yield, dividend, futures roll, etc.). FX carry: interest rate differential. Commodity carry: backwardation. Bond carry: yield-curve slope.
  • Value. Long undervalued assets (low price relative to fundamental anchor: P/E, P/B, REER). The mean-reversion premium.
  • Momentum. Long recent winners, short recent losers. Time-series or cross-sectional. The trend premium.
  • Defensive. Long low-beta or low-vol assets. The "betting against beta" premium.

These four work in equities, bonds, commodities, FX, with surprising robustness over 100+ years. Not all are present in all assets, but the intersection is non-empty for any major liquid market.

3. Equilibrium returns and reverse optimisation

If you have no signal, what is the best estimate of $\mu$? Not the historical mean (too noisy), not zero (markets do offer premia). The clean answer: the implied returns from holding the market portfolio at equilibrium.

$$\Pi = \lambda \Sigma w_m$$

where $w_m$ is the market-cap weight vector and $\lambda$ is the representative investor's risk aversion. This is the Black-Litterman prior (Lesson 11). It is internally consistent: optimising on $\Pi$ gives back $w_m$.

Most practitioners start with $\Pi$ as the prior $\mu$, then add view shifts from macro signals. This is exactly the Black-Litterman pipeline previewed here.

4. Macro signals as conditional expected returns

Macrosynergy's framework: build a "conditional expected return" by combining a small set of theoretically motivated macro signals.

For FX: relative growth differential, real interest rate differential, current account balance, terms of trade. Each scored as a z-score within a panel and then equal-weighted into a composite.

For rates: real yield level, fiscal stance, growth nowcast, inflation expectations.

For equity country allocation: macro-quantamental scorecard with 5 to 10 themes (art 15).

The key principle (recurring throughout MS): each signal is constructed from theoretical priors, not learned from in-sample backtest performance. Equal-weighted composite. No optimisation.

5. IC, IR, Grinold's law

The information coefficient measures signal quality:

$$\text{IC} = \text{Corr}(s_t, r_{t+1})$$

computed cross-sectionally (across assets at each date) or time-serially (across dates for one asset).

The information ratio is the ex-ante alpha per unit of active risk:

$$\text{IR} \approx \text{IC} \cdot \sqrt{\text{breadth}}$$

Grinold's "fundamental law of active management" (1989). Doubles your IC, doubles your IR. Quadruples your breadth, doubles your IR. Combining many low-IC signals on many assets is the only way to get high IR.

6. Signal-to-position mapping

Once you have a composite signal, you need to map it to a position. Macrosynergy's conventions (art 5, 15):

  • Sequential z-score. For each date and each asset, compute $z = (s - \mu_s) / \sigma_s$ using only data available at $t$.
  • Capping. Clip $z$ at $\pm 2$ or $\pm 3$ to avoid blowups from outliers.
  • Sigmoid sizing. Map the capped z to a position via $w \propto 2 / (1 + e^{-k z}) - 1$. Smooth, differentiable, no discrete jumps.
  • Vol targeting. Multiply by $\sigma_{\text{target}} / \sigma_{\text{recent}}$ at the asset level.
In practice (Macrosynergy art 38, 50, 65): The MS signal stack is the building blocks of every published strategy. Article 38 frames the "macro quantamental" approach. Article 50 gives the three-step protocol for evaluating any signal: (1) predictive correlation, (2) accuracy/hit-rate, (3) naive PnL. Article 65 expands this with quality diagnostics. The conceptual parity philosophy: equal-weight signals within a theme, equal-weight themes within an asset, equal-weight assets within a strategy. Never optimise the weights, even when in-sample tests favour optimisation.

6b. Deep dive: style premia performance, century by century

The "style premia" framework rests on a simple empirical claim: carry, value, momentum, and defensive deliver positive Sharpe across asset classes and across decades. AQR's "Trends Everywhere" (Hurst, Ooi, Pedersen 2017) tested this on 100+ years. The numbers are surprisingly stable.

The combined Sharpe

Combining four styles equally across four asset classes gives roughly Sharpe 1.5 to 2.0 in raw backtests. Realistic implementation Sharpe (after costs, deflated, capacity-adjusted): 0.7 to 1.0. AQR's Style Premia Alternative Fund and similar products run in this range.

Why these survive

Three theories, all with empirical support:

  • Risk premium. Each style is bearing a real risk that risk-averse investors pay you to bear. Value: bankruptcy risk. Carry: tail risk. Momentum: crash risk. Defensive: behavioural overweighting of lottery-like high-beta.
  • Behavioural. Humans systematically misjudge value (extrapolate growth), momentum (reversals), defensive (overconfidence). Slow-moving capital can not arbitrage these away.
  • Structural. Mandate constraints prevent some investors from accessing the strategies. Long-only mandates leave shorts unexploited. Pension fund constraints leave low-beta unfunded.

7. Mathematical companion

7.1 Why the historical mean is a terrible $\mu$

The standard error of the sample mean of returns is $\sigma / \sqrt{T}$. For monthly returns at 15% annualised vol over 30 years, the standard error is $0.15 / \sqrt{30} \approx 2.7\%$. So your point estimate is $\hat \mu \pm 5.4\%$ (95% CI). That's bigger than most premia. The historical mean is the maximum likelihood estimator, but its sampling variance is so large that it's useless for portfolio optimisation.

7.2 Reverse optimisation derivation

Mean-variance solution: $w^\star = (\lambda \Sigma)^{-1} \mu$. Set $w^\star = w_m$ (the market). Solve for $\mu$: $\mu = \lambda \Sigma w_m$. This is the equilibrium prior, automatically internally consistent.

7.3 The IC-IR identity

For a signal-based strategy with cross-sectional rank correlation IC and breadth $N$, the active risk-adjusted return is approximately

$$\text{IR} \approx \text{IC} \cdot \sqrt{N}$$

under the assumption that signals across the $N$ assets are independent and unbiased. The proof is a CLT applied to the sum of independent signal contributions. Grinold and Kahn (2000) give the formal derivation.

7.4 Sigmoid vs threshold position sizing

Linear sizing $w = z$ is unbounded; large signals create huge positions. Threshold sizing $w = \mathbb{1}(z > c)$ has discrete jumps and large turnover near the threshold. Sigmoid $w = \tanh(k z)$ caps positions smoothly. Choose $k$ such that $\tanh(k \cdot 2) \approx 0.95$, so a 2-sigma signal hits 95% of max position.

7.5 Signal decay and rebalancing frequency

If signal $s$ has autocorrelation $\rho$ at the rebalancing frequency, a fraction $1 - \rho$ of the position must be turned over each period. Annual turnover $\approx N \cdot (1 - \rho)$ where $N$ is the number of rebalances per year. Slow signals: fewer rebalances. Fast signals: more rebalances and more cost.

8. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: Z-score within date

def zscore_within_date(signal: pd.DataFrame) -> pd.DataFrame:
    return signal.sub(signal.mean(axis=1), axis=0).div(signal.std(axis=1), axis=0)

Exercise B: Sigmoid sizing

def sigmoid_position(z, k=1.0, max_pos=1.0):
    return max_pos * np.tanh(k * np.clip(z, -3, 3))

Exercise C: Information coefficient

def information_coefficient(signal: pd.DataFrame, returns: pd.DataFrame) -> pd.Series:
    """Cross-sectional rank IC at each date."""
    return signal.corrwith(returns.shift(-1), axis=1, method='spearman')

Exercise D: Equilibrium returns

def implied_returns(w_market, Sigma, lambda_=2.5):
    return lambda_ * Sigma @ w_market

9. Self test

Question 1, conceptual

Why does Grinold's law require the signals to be independent? What happens when you stack 5 highly correlated signals?

Question 2, coding

Build a carry signal for 5 currencies (EUR, JPY, GBP, AUD, NZD vs USD). Use the interest rate differential as the signal. Z-score within date. Sigmoid to positions. Backtest 10 years.

Question 3, numerical

For IC = 0.08, breadth = 100, what is the implied IR? What if you can only execute 50 of those 100 bets due to liquidity? Compare.
