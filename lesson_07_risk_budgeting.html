0. Reading the controls

Risk budgets $b_i$

The vector of target risk shares. $b_i$ is the fraction of total portfolio risk that asset $i$ should carry. Constraints: $b_i \ge 0$ and $\sum_i b_i = 1$. Equal risk contribution means $b_i = 1/N$.

Asset volatilities $\sigma_i$

Same as previous lessons. Annualised. The risk-budgeting algorithm uses individual vols and correlations.

1. Risk decomposition by asset

The portfolio variance is $w^\top \Sigma w$. By Euler's theorem applied to homogeneous-of-degree-2 functions:

$$w^\top \Sigma w = \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \cdot (\Sigma w)_i$$

Each term $w_i (\Sigma w)_i$ is the risk contribution of asset $i$. They sum to total variance, exactly. Divide by portfolio standard deviation $\sigma_p = \sqrt{w^\top \Sigma w}$:

$$\sigma_p = \sum_{i=1}^N \text{RC}_i, \quad \text{RC}_i = \frac{w_i (\Sigma w)_i}{\sigma_p}$$

The $\text{RC}_i$ sum to portfolio vol. They are dollar-units of risk per asset, with no double counting and no leftovers. This is the cleanest way to attribute risk.

2. Marginal, incremental, component

Three related quantities, often confused.

Marginal contribution $\text{MC}_i = \partial \sigma_p / \partial w_i = (\Sigma w)_i / \sigma_p$. The change in portfolio vol per unit increase in $w_i$. Linear, additive in the sense $\sigma_p = \sum_i w_i \text{MC}_i$.

Component contribution $\text{CC}_i = w_i \text{MC}_i$. Same as $\text{RC}_i$. Each asset's "share" of portfolio vol.

Incremental contribution $\text{IC}_i = \sigma_p(w) - \sigma_p(w \text{ without asset } i)$. The change in portfolio vol from removing asset $i$ entirely. Generally not equal to $\text{CC}_i$ unless $\Sigma$ is diagonal.

3. The diversification ratio

Choueifaty and Coignard (2008) define

$$\text{DR}(w) = \frac{w^\top \sigma}{\sqrt{w^\top \Sigma w}} = \frac{\text{weighted average vol}}{\text{portfolio vol}}$$

where $\sigma$ is the vector of individual volatilities. Always $\ge 1$. Single-asset portfolios have DR $= 1$. Equal-weight on uncorrelated assets approaches DR $= \sqrt{N}$. The "Maximum Diversification Portfolio" (MDP) maximises DR over the feasible set.

4. The risk budgeting problem

Given a target budget vector $b$ with $\sum b_i = 1$, find $w$ such that $\text{RC}_i / \sigma_p = b_i$ for all $i$. Equivalently:

$$w_i (\Sigma w)_i = b_i (w^\top \Sigma w) \quad \forall i$$

This is a system of $N$ nonlinear equations. Maillard, Roncalli, Teiletche (2010) show it has a unique solution under positivity ($w_i > 0$). Spinu (2013) gives a convex reformulation:

$$\min_{w > 0} \tfrac{1}{2} w^\top \Sigma w - \sum_i b_i \log w_i$$

Standard convex solvers handle this. For ERC ($b_i = 1/N$), the solution is the Equal Risk Contribution portfolio.

5. Equal risk contribution and risk parity

ERC is the special case $b_i = 1/N$. Each asset contributes exactly $1/N$ of total portfolio variance. Properties:

  • No $\mu$ required. Robust to estimation error in expected returns.
  • Always sits between equal-weight and minimum variance.
  • Out-of-sample, often beats both Markowitz and equal-weight (DeMiguel et al., 2009 finding extends here).
  • Sharpe is naturally lower than the tangency portfolio (no return prediction). ERC needs leverage to match equity Sharpe.

Risk parity is the asset-allocation strategy built on ERC: scale up the bond exposure with leverage so its risk contribution matches equity. Pioneered by Bridgewater All Weather (1996) and AQR Risk Parity (2006). Lesson 14 covers the implementation in detail.

In practice (Macrosynergy art 23, 88): Macro-aware risk parity. Take ERC weights as the baseline and tilt them with a macro signal (e.g. growth, inflation regime) to capture cyclical opportunities. Article 23 demonstrates this on cross-asset data with Sharpe 1.3 (vs 0.8 for plain risk parity). Article 88 generalises with a multi-factor macro overlay. The key insight: ERC gives you a stable risk-balanced portfolio; the macro tilt adds the alpha. This is a deliberate "two-fund" structure where one fund is the risk-balanced book and the other is the macro overlay.

5b. Deep dive: the 60/40 risk illusion

The most common asset allocation in the world is "60/40" - 60% equities, 40% bonds. Investors think of it as a balanced portfolio. In risk space it is anything but balanced.

The numbers

Typical inputs: SPY at $\sigma_e = 16\%$, AGG at $\sigma_b = 6\%$, $\rho = -0.10$.

Portfolio variance: $0.6^2 \cdot 0.16^2 + 0.4^2 \cdot 0.06^2 + 2 \cdot 0.6 \cdot 0.4 \cdot (-0.10) \cdot 0.16 \cdot 0.06$.

= 0.009216 + 0.000576 - 0.000461 = 0.009331.

Portfolio vol = 9.66%.

Risk contributions: $\text{RC}_e = 0.6 \cdot (\Sigma w)_1 / \sigma_p$. The numerator $(\Sigma w)_1 = 0.16^2 \cdot 0.6 + (-0.10) \cdot 0.16 \cdot 0.06 \cdot 0.4 = 0.01536 - 0.000384 = 0.014976$.

$\text{RC}_e = 0.6 \cdot 0.014976 / 0.0966 = 0.0930$. That's 96% of total vol (0.0966).

Bond contribution: 4%.

The headline

A "balanced" 60/40 portfolio is 96/4 in risk space. This is why 60/40 is essentially an equity bet with a small bond hedge. It's why risk parity (Lesson 14) was invented.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Why Euler's theorem applies

$f(w) = w^\top \Sigma w$ is homogeneous of degree 2: $f(c w) = c^2 f(w)$. By Euler's theorem, $\sum_i w_i (\partial f / \partial w_i) = 2 f(w)$. The factor of 2 disappears when we work with $\sigma_p = \sqrt{f(w)}$, which is homogeneous of degree 1. The risk-contribution decomposition is exact: no remainder, no approximation.

6.2 The Spinu convex reformulation

The risk-budgeting problem $w_i (\Sigma w)_i = b_i (w^\top \Sigma w)$ has the same first-order conditions as $\min_{w > 0} \tfrac{1}{2} w^\top \Sigma w - \sum_i b_i \log w_i$. The log barrier creates a unique interior solution. Coordinate descent converges quickly: at each step, fix all but one $w_i$ and solve a 1D quadratic.

6.3 Effective number of bets (Meucci)

Meucci (2009) proposes the entropy of the risk-contribution share vector:

$$\text{ENB} = \exp\left(- \sum_i \frac{\text{RC}_i}{\sigma_p} \log \frac{\text{RC}_i}{\sigma_p}\right)$$

ENB ranges from 1 (single-asset concentration) to $N$ (perfect ERC). It is invariant to correlation structure when applied to the principal-component projection.

6.4 Risk parity vs minimum variance: when do they coincide?

If all assets have equal volatility and equal pairwise correlations, ERC and min-var both produce equal weights. They diverge when $\Sigma$ has heterogeneous diagonals. ERC weights inversely with vol (so low-vol assets get big weights) but adjusts for correlations (so heavily-correlated low-vol assets get less weight than naive inverse-vol).

6.5 The "leverage problem" of risk parity

To match a 60/40 portfolio's expected return at the same vol, ERC typically needs 1.5x to 2x leverage. The leverage cost (financing rate above the risk-free rate) eats into the Sharpe gain. Asness, Frazzini, Pedersen (2012) find that risk parity beats 60/40 even after realistic leverage costs, but the margin shrinks. The 2022 inflation shock hurt risk parity disproportionately because both bonds and equities sold off, breaking the "hedge" assumption.

def risk_contributions(w, Sigma):
    sigma_p = np.sqrt(w @ Sigma @ w)
    mc = (Sigma @ w) / sigma_p
    rc = w * mc
    return {'sigma_p': sigma_p, 'mc': mc, 'rc': rc, 'share': rc / sigma_p}

Exercise C: Risk budgeting via convex optimisation

import cvxpy as cp

def risk_budget_portfolio(Sigma, b):
    n = Sigma.shape[0]
    w = cp.Variable(n, pos=True)
    obj = cp.Minimize(0.5 * cp.quad_form(w, Sigma) - b @ cp.log(w))
    prob = cp.Problem(obj)
    prob.solve()
    return w.value / w.value.sum()

8. Self test

Question 1, conceptual

For a 60/40 portfolio (60% S&P 500 at 18% vol, 40% AGG at 6% vol, $\rho = 0$), compute each asset's risk contribution share. Why is the share so far from 60/40?

Answer: equity vol $\times$ 0.6 = 10.8; bond vol $\times$ 0.4 = 2.4. Total $\approx \sqrt{10.8^2 + 2.4^2} \approx 11.06$. Share: equity 97%, bond 3%. The "60/40" portfolio is actually 97/3 in risk space.
