0. Reading the controls: what every slider means

Confidence level $\alpha$

The probability that a loss is at or below VaR. $\alpha = 0.95$ means "the loss I will not exceed on 95% of days". The complement, $1 - \alpha = 5\%$, is the tail mass we are pricing.

Three industry-standard levels:

  • $\alpha = 0.95$ (one bad day in 20). Used in trading desks for daily P and L limits.
  • $\alpha = 0.99$ (one in 100). The Basel Committee VaR standard.
  • $\alpha = 0.999$ (one in 1000). Used for stressed-VaR and capital adequacy. Almost always combined with stress scenarios because the data sample is too small to estimate this level reliably.

The slider goes from 0.80 to 0.999. As $\alpha \to 1$, your sample size effectively shrinks: at $\alpha = 0.999$ on 2520 days of data, you have only 2 to 3 observations in the tail. Estimates become unstable.

Sample size $n$

Number of daily observations. The standard error of an empirical quantile shrinks as $1 / \sqrt{n}$, but only after $n$ is large enough to actually contain enough tail observations. As a rule of thumb, $n \cdot (1 - \alpha) \ge 30$ for the empirical quantile to be reliable. At $\alpha = 0.99$, that means $n \ge 3000$ or roughly 12 years of daily data.

Distribution method

  • Historical. Empirical quantile of the observed losses. Non-parametric. Robust to model misspecification but suffers when the future does not look like the past.
  • Gaussian fit. Estimate $\mu, \sigma$ from data, then compute VaR analytically as $-\mu + \sigma z_\alpha$. Smooth and stable. Catastrophically underestimates tail risk for fat-tailed assets.
  • Student t fit. Estimate $\mu, \sigma, \text{df}$ from data, then compute VaR using the t quantile. The middle ground: parametric and stable, but flexible enough to capture heavy tails.

Asset profile parameters

Each asset profile uses a Student t with characteristic df: SPY df = 5, AGG df = 8, BTC df = 3, 60/40 mix df = 6. Lower df means heavier tails (see Section 9 below).

Default probability $p$ (counterexample demo)

The probability per period that a single bond defaults. We use this in the section that demonstrates VaR's failure of sub-additivity. The key insight: when $p$ sits in the interval $(1 - \alpha, \alpha)$, the bond's individual VaR is zero but the portfolio of two bonds has positive VaR. That is the sub-additivity violation.

Vol persistence $\beta$ (drawdown demo)

The GARCH persistence parameter. Same role as in Lesson 1. Higher $\beta$ creates longer vol clusters, which compound into deeper drawdowns even when $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are held constant. This is the cleanest empirical reason that drawdown is a path-dependent measure that distributional measures (variance, VaR, CVaR) miss.

Random seed (drawdown demo)

Same idea as Lesson 1. Each seed gives one realisation from the same probability law. Slide the seed and watch how max drawdown can vary by a factor of 2 to 3 across realisations of the same process. Drawdown is a single-path statistic with high sampling variance. One backtest's max drawdown is a noisy estimate of the true max drawdown distribution.

1. Why variance is not enough

Variance treats upside and downside symmetrically. A 30% gain and a 30% loss contribute identically to $\sigma^2$. For a portfolio, this is rarely what you want. Investors care about losses, not deviations.

Variance also gives no information about the tail shape. Two distributions can have identical variance and very different tail risk: a Gaussian with $\sigma = 10\%$ and a Student t with $\sigma = 10\%$, $\text{df} = 4$ have the same standard deviation but the t has roughly 3 times the probability mass beyond 3 sigma.

The fix is to define risk measures that look directly at the loss distribution. We will build three: VaR, CVaR, and drawdown.

2. Value at Risk

VaR is a quantile of the loss distribution. The 95% VaR is the loss you would not exceed on 95% of days. Formally:

$$\text{VaR}_\alpha(L) = \inf\{x : P(L \le x) \ge \alpha\}$$

where $L$ is the loss (positive numbers mean losses). This is just the $\alpha$-th quantile of $L$. If $\alpha = 0.95$, you are looking for the loss that exceeds 95% of all daily losses, i.e., the 5% worst tail boundary.

Three common conventions: $\alpha = 0.95$ (one bad day in 20), $\alpha = 0.99$ (one in 100), $\alpha = 0.999$ (one in 1000, used in regulatory capital). The right choice depends on what you are reserving capital for.

3. Conditional VaR (CVaR)

CVaR, also called Expected Shortfall, is the average loss given that you are past the VaR threshold. Formally:

$$\text{CVaR}_\alpha(L) = \mathbb{E}[L \mid L \ge \text{VaR}_\alpha(L)]$$

For a continuous distribution this can be written as

$$\text{CVaR}_\alpha(L) = \frac{1}{1 - \alpha} \int_\alpha^1 \text{VaR}_u(L) \, du$$

which says CVaR is the average of all VaR levels above $\alpha$. Empirically, you compute it by averaging the worst $(1 - \alpha)$ fraction of losses.

For a Gaussian loss with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$, CVaR has a closed form:

$$\text{CVaR}^{\text{Gaussian}}_\alpha = \mu + \sigma \frac{\phi(z_\alpha)}{1 - \alpha}$$

where $\phi$ is the standard normal density and $z_\alpha = \Phi^{-1}(\alpha)$. At $\alpha = 0.99$, the ratio CVaR / VaR is about 1.15 for a Gaussian. For a Student t with df = 4, the ratio rises to about 1.4. Heavy tails make CVaR much larger than VaR.

4. Coherence: the four axioms

Artzner, Delbaen, Eber, and Heath (1999) proposed four axioms that any "real" risk measure should satisfy. Let $\rho$ denote a risk measure (positive numbers mean more risk).

1. Monotonicity. If portfolio $X$ always loses at least as much as portfolio $Y$, then $\rho(X) \ge \rho(Y)$. Riskier portfolios have higher risk numbers.
2. Translation invariance. $\rho(X + c) = \rho(X) - c$. Adding cash $c$ reduces risk one-for-one.
3. Positive homogeneity. $\rho(\lambda X) = \lambda \rho(X)$ for $\lambda \ge 0$. Doubling exposure doubles risk.
4. Sub-additivity. $\rho(X + Y) \le \rho(X) + \rho(Y)$. Combining portfolios cannot increase total risk. Diversification cannot hurt.

A risk measure that satisfies all four is called coherent. Variance is not coherent (it fails translation invariance and positive homogeneity, although volatility $\sigma$ is positively homogeneous). VaR is not coherent in general (it fails sub-additivity). CVaR is coherent. This is the formal reason CVaR is preferred for portfolio optimisation.

5. Why VaR fails sub-additivity, with a worked example

Here is a concrete two-portfolio example. Suppose each of two zero-coupon bonds has a 4% chance of defaulting. Each pays nothing if it defaults and \$100 if it does not. Losses (relative to the par value of \$100):

  • Bond A alone: $P(L = 100) = 0.04$, $P(L = 0) = 0.96$.
  • The 95% VaR of bond A is \$0 (the 5th percentile from the top of the loss distribution still falls in the no-default region).

Same for bond B independently. Now combine them, equal weights, so each contributes \$50 to the portfolio:

  • Joint distribution under independence: $P(\text{neither defaults}) = 0.9216$, $P(\text{exactly one}) = 0.0768$, $P(\text{both}) = 0.0016$.
  • Loss of \$50 has probability 7.68%. Loss of \$100 has probability 0.16%.
  • The 95% VaR is now \$50 (since 7.68% of paths have a \$50 loss, this exceeds the 5% threshold).

So $\text{VaR}_{0.95}(A) = 0$, $\text{VaR}_{0.95}(B) = 0$, but $\text{VaR}_{0.95}(A + B) = 50$. Sub-additivity is violated: combining two zero-VaR positions creates positive VaR. Diversification appears to increase risk under VaR. CVaR does not have this problem.

6. Drawdown, the path-dependent measure

Variance, VaR, CVaR all look at the marginal distribution of returns. They ignore order. Drawdown is different. The drawdown at time $t$ is the percentage loss from the peak so far:

$$\text{DD}_t = 1 - \frac{W_t}{\max_{s \le t} W_s}$$

Maximum drawdown is the worst drawdown over the whole path:

$$\text{MDD} = \max_t \text{DD}_t$$

Drawdown matters for two reasons. Psychological: investors panic and redeem at large drawdowns. Mechanical: a 50% drawdown requires a 100% gain to recover, so deep drawdowns destroy compound growth even if average returns recover.

Two return distributions with the same variance and same VaR can have wildly different drawdowns depending on the autocorrelation of negative returns. Volatility clustering (Lesson 1 stylised fact 2) is what makes real-market drawdowns so much worse than IID-Gaussian theory predicts.

Drawdown control is one of the few risk frameworks that pays for itself.

7. Choosing the right measure

Use variance / volatility when you have many assets, returns are roughly Gaussian, and you need a smooth optimisable objective. Markowitz mean-variance (Lesson 5) requires it. Rebalancing frequencies of weekly to monthly with diversified exposure usually work.

Use VaR when regulators or risk-limit systems demand it. Most banks operate under VaR limits because of historical regulatory inertia. Be aware of its non-coherence and back-test exceedances.

Use CVaR for portfolio optimisation involving tail-sensitive assets: options, credit, illiquid private. The Rockafellar-Uryasev LP formulation (Lesson 16) makes this just as tractable as mean-variance.

Use maximum drawdown / Calmar / CDaR when investor behaviour matters: hedge fund redemptions, retail flows, career-risk for portfolio managers. Drawdowns drive funding behaviour even when statistical risk metrics look fine.

Use multiple measures together. Best practice in production risk systems is to compute all of them and flag any that breach a threshold. Each measure captures something the others miss.

Python translation

import numpy as np

def historical_var_cvar(losses, alpha=0.95):
    """Empirical VaR and CVaR at confidence alpha."""
    losses = np.asarray(losses)
    var = np.quantile(losses, alpha)
    cvar = losses[losses >= var].mean()
    return {'var': var, 'cvar': cvar}

def max_drawdown(equity):
    """Max drawdown of an equity curve, plus dates."""
    eq = np.asarray(equity)
    peak = np.maximum.accumulate(eq)
    dd = 1 - eq / peak
    trough_i = int(np.argmax(dd))
    peak_i = int(np.argmax(eq[:trough_i + 1]))
    return {'max_dd': float(dd[trough_i]), 'peak_idx': peak_i, 'trough_idx': trough_i}

def cdar(equity, alpha=0.95):
    """Conditional drawdown at risk."""
    eq = np.asarray(equity)
    peak = np.maximum.accumulate(eq)
    dd = 1 - eq / peak
    threshold = np.quantile(dd, alpha)
    tail = dd[dd >= threshold]
    return float(tail.mean())
In practice (Macrosynergy production stack): Volatility targeting at the strategy level + MinMax drawdown control at the portfolio level. CVaR is computed for academic comparison and stress reporting but is rarely the primary optimisation objective. The reason is empirical: vol targets and drawdown stops respond fast to market changes (within a week of a regime shift), while a parametric CVaR depends on a fixed loss distribution that goes stale during regime transitions. The combined stack is documented in articles 5 (FX), 13 (EM trend), and 220 (drawdown), and applied implicitly in every published strategy. Lessons 21 and 16 in this curriculum build the two layers respectively.

8. Mathematical companion: derivations and proofs

8.1 Why CVaR is sub-additive (and VaR is not)

Sub-additivity says $\rho(X + Y) \le \rho(X) + \rho(Y)$. For CVaR the proof rests on its representation as an average of quantiles:

$$\text{CVaR}_\alpha(L) = \frac{1}{1 - \alpha} \int_\alpha^1 \text{VaR}_u(L) \, du$$

And the dual representation

$$\text{CVaR}_\alpha(L) = \sup_{Q \in \mathcal{Q}_\alpha} \mathbb{E}_Q[L]$$

where $\mathcal{Q}_\alpha$ is a set of probability measures absolutely continuous with respect to the original, with bounded Radon-Nikodym derivative. CVaR is a supremum of linear functionals of the loss, and a supremum of sub-additive functionals is itself sub-additive.

VaR fails because it picks a single quantile rather than averaging the tail. The two-bond example in Section 5 above is a literal counterexample. Mathematically, the failure is tied to the discontinuity of the indicator function $\mathbb{1}\{L \ge \text{VaR}\}$ in the loss distribution.

8.2 Translation invariance and "cash-additivity"

$\rho(X + c) = \rho(X) - c$ for any constant $c$. The interpretation: adding $c$ in cash to a portfolio reduces its risk by exactly $c$. This is what makes $\rho$ measure risk in monetary units. Without it, doubling cash would not halve risk in any meaningful way.

Variance does not satisfy translation invariance: $\text{Var}(X + c) = \text{Var}(X)$. Adding cash leaves variance unchanged. This is one reason variance is not by itself a complete risk measure for capital adequacy purposes.

8.3 The acceptance set framework

Artzner et al. (1999) showed that any coherent risk measure can be written as

$$\rho(X) = \inf\{m \in \mathbb{R} : X + m \in \mathcal{A}\}$$

where $\mathcal{A}$ is a set of "acceptable" portfolios. The risk of $X$ is the smallest cash addition that makes $X$ acceptable. Different acceptance sets give different coherent measures. CVaR corresponds to "the average loss conditional on tail event must be no worse than $-c$". Worst-case scenario optimisation corresponds to "no path under any scenario worse than $-c$". This unifies many practical risk frameworks.

8.4 VaR backtesting (Kupiec and Christoffersen)

If your model says $\text{VaR}_\alpha = v$, you should observe losses exceeding $v$ on a fraction $1 - \alpha$ of days. Two formal tests:

Kupiec's POF test. Under the null hypothesis that the model is correct, the number of exceedances $N$ follows Binomial$(n, 1 - \alpha)$. The likelihood ratio test statistic

$$LR_{\text{POF}} = -2 \ln\left(\frac{(1-\alpha)^N \alpha^{n-N}}{(N/n)^N (1 - N/n)^{n-N}}\right)$$

follows a chi-squared with 1 df under the null. Reject if $LR > 3.84$ (95% level).

Christoffersen's IID test. Exceedances should be independent across days. If they cluster, you have a regime-detection problem on top of unconditional miscalibration.

Combined test: Christoffersen (1998) gives a joint test of correct unconditional coverage and independence.

8.5 Drawdown vs distributional measures

Variance, VaR, CVaR all depend only on the marginal distribution of single-period returns. They are permutation-invariant: shuffle the order of returns, the metric is unchanged. Drawdown is not permutation-invariant. The same set of returns in different orders produces different drawdowns. The order matters because peaks and troughs depend on partial sums.

Formally, drawdown is a functional of the equity curve $W_t = W_0 \prod_{s \le t}(1 + r_s)$, and any permutation of returns changes which partial sums are minima.

Practical consequence. A portfolio that loses 1% per day for 30 days has the same monthly variance as a portfolio that gains 1% per day for 15 days then loses 1% per day for 15 days. They have very different drawdowns. Volatility clustering (Lesson 1) makes the bad-day-clustering scenario likelier in real markets, which is why realised drawdowns exceed iid-Gaussian predictions.

8.6 The Calmar, Sterling, and Burke ratios

Drawdown-based reward-risk ratios. Each one normalises return by a different summary of the drawdown path.

  • Calmar = annualised return / max drawdown. Most common.
  • Sterling = annualised return / (average of $N$ largest drawdowns minus 10%). Smooths Calmar.
  • Burke = annualised return / sqrt(sum of squared drawdowns). Penalises many medium drawdowns more than one large one.

None are coherent risk measures in the Artzner sense, but they are widely used because they speak the language of investors who care about peak-to-trough pain.

8.7 Why do regulators prefer VaR over CVaR? (and the slow change)

For two decades, Basel II used VaR at 99% confidence. CVaR was known to be theoretically superior (coherent, captures tail expectation) but had practical disadvantages: harder to backtest with finite data, more sensitive to model assumptions, and less intuitive ("expected loss in the worst 1%" is a more abstract object than "the 99th percentile loss").

Basel III, finalised in 2017 and rolled out 2023 to 2025, switches to Expected Shortfall (= CVaR) at 97.5% confidence. The 97.5% level for ES roughly matches the historical 99% VaR in normal markets but captures more of the tail in stress regimes. The lesson: regulatory practice catches up to academic theory, but slowly.

8.8 Connection to expected utility theory

Variance optimisation is justified by quadratic utility, which has the unwanted property of being non-monotone (more wealth eventually decreases utility, since $u(w) = w - \frac{a}{2}w^2$ has $u'(w) = 1 - aw$ negative for large $w$). This is one philosophical reason variance is not a satisfying risk measure for very wealthy or very poor investors.

CVaR optimisation can be derived from non-expected-utility frameworks like Yaari's dual theory or rank-dependent expected utility. These value tail outcomes more than expected utility does, matching the observed behaviour of investors who buy insurance and worry about disasters.

Exercise A: Filtered historical simulation VaR

The bridge between historical and parametric: standardise returns by their conditional volatility (from EWMA or GARCH), then sample from the standardised distribution and rescale by today's vol. Captures fat tails and clustering.

def filtered_historical_var(returns, alpha=0.99, ewma_lambda=0.94):
    var_t = np.zeros(len(returns))
    var_t[0] = returns.var(ddof=1)
    for t in range(1, len(returns)):
        var_t[t] = ewma_lambda * var_t[t-1] + (1 - ewma_lambda) * returns.iloc[t-1] ** 2
    sigma_t = np.sqrt(var_t)
    standardised = returns / sigma_t
    quantile = np.quantile(-standardised, alpha)
    return quantile * sigma_t[-1]

Exercise B: Cornish-Fisher VaR

Adjust the Gaussian quantile for skew and kurtosis. Pragmatic middle ground between Gaussian and full historical.

def cornish_fisher_var(returns, alpha=0.99):
    mu, sd = -returns.mean(), returns.std(ddof=1)
    z = stats.norm.ppf(alpha)
    s = stats.skew(returns)
    k = stats.kurtosis(returns, fisher=True)
    z_cf = z + (z**2 - 1) * s / 6 + (z**3 - 3*z) * k / 24 - (2*z**3 - 5*z) * s**2 / 36
    return mu + sd * z_cf

Exercise C: Christoffersen IID test for VaR exceedances

Tests independence: are exceedance days clustered? If yes, the model misses regime dynamics.

def christoffersen_iid_test(exceedances):
    """exceedances: 1D boolean array indicating VaR breaches."""
    n00 = n01 = n10 = n11 = 0
    for t in range(1, len(exceedances)):
        if not exceedances[t-1] and not exceedances[t]: n00 += 1
        if not exceedances[t-1] and exceedances[t]:     n01 += 1
        if exceedances[t-1] and not exceedances[t]:     n10 += 1
        if exceedances[t-1] and exceedances[t]:         n11 += 1
    p01 = n01 / (n00 + n01) if (n00 + n01) > 0 else 0
    p11 = n11 / (n10 + n11) if (n10 + n11) > 0 else 0
    p = (n01 + n11) / (n00 + n01 + n10 + n11)
    if p in (0, 1) or p01 in (0, 1) or p11 in (0, 1): return None
    log_l_null = (n00 + n10) * np.log(1 - p) + (n01 + n11) * np.log(p)
    log_l_alt = n00 * np.log(1 - p01) + n01 * np.log(p01) + n10 * np.log(1 - p11) + n11 * np.log(p11)
    lr = -2 * (log_l_null - log_l_alt)
    return dict(lr=lr, reject=lr > 3.84)

Exercise D: Rolling drawdown for risk monitoring

Maximum drawdown over a rolling window. Useful for risk dashboards: alarm when 6-month rolling drawdown exceeds X%.

def rolling_max_drawdown(equity, window=126):
    """126 trading days = 6 months. Returns a Series of rolling max drawdowns."""
    eq = equity.values
    out = np.full(len(eq), np.nan)
    for t in range(window, len(eq)):
        win = eq[t - window:t + 1]
        peak = np.maximum.accumulate(win)
        dd = 1 - win / peak
        out[t] = dd.max()
    return pd.Series(out, index=equity.index)

Exercise E: Risk-adjusted performance attribution

Decompose a portfolio return into Sharpe-equivalent contributions per asset.

def sharpe_attribution(weights, returns_df, rf=0.02 / 252):
    daily_rets = returns_df @ weights
    sigma = daily_rets.std(ddof=1) * np.sqrt(252)
    contributions = {}
    for col in returns_df.columns:
        contrib_to_mean = weights[col] * (returns_df[col].mean() - rf) * 252
        contrib_to_vol = (weights[col] * (returns_df.cov() @ weights)[col]) * 252 / sigma
        contributions[col] = contrib_to_mean / sigma
    return pd.Series(contributions)

10. Self test

Question 1, conceptual

An options trader writes deep out-of-the-money puts. The premium is small but losses on a crash can be enormous. Which risk measure is most misleading for this position, and which is most informative? Explain.

Answer: Variance is misleading because day-to-day variation is small until a crash. VaR can be misleading at common confidence levels because the tail event sits past the VaR boundary, contributing zero. CVaR captures the expected loss conditional on the crash, which is the right number. Drawdown captures the path of the crash. Use CVaR + max drawdown together.

Question 2, numerical

For a Gaussian loss with $\mu = 0$ and $\sigma = 1$, compute VaR$_{0.99}$ and CVaR$_{0.99}$ by hand. Use $z_{0.99} = 2.33$ and $\phi(2.33) = 0.0264$. Confirm CVaR / VaR ≈ 1.15.

Answer: VaR = 2.33. CVaR = $\phi(2.33) / (1 - 0.99)$ = $0.0264 / 0.01$ = $2.64$. Ratio = $2.64 / 2.33$ = $1.13$.

Extend historical_var_cvar to handle a 2D array of asset losses (rows = days, cols = assets). Return a Series of VaR and CVaR per asset. Then run it on a 60/40 portfolio: VaR(60/40) should be smaller than 0.6 VaR(equity) + 0.4 VaR(bond) most of the time, satisfying CVaR sub-additivity.
