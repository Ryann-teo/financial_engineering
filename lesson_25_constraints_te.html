0. Reading the controls

Tracking error budget (% per year)

How much active risk you allow. Index funds: 0.1 to 0.5%. Closet indexers: 1 to 2%. Active long-only: 3 to 6%. Hedge funds: 6 to 15%.

Beta target

Portfolio beta to benchmark. 1.0 means market-neutral exposure to benchmark; 0 means market-neutral. Long-short funds typically target 0 to 0.3.

Sector cap

Max active weight per sector. Common: 5% per sector overweight or underweight relative to benchmark.

1. Active weights and tracking error

Define active weight $a = w - w_b$ where $w_b$ is the benchmark. The active return is $a^\top r$ and the tracking error (TE) is

$$\text{TE} = \sqrt{a^\top \Sigma a}$$

Properties:

  • $\sum_i a_i = 0$ (active weights sum to zero by construction).
  • TE $\to 0$ as $w \to w_b$.
  • For a long-only fund, $a_i \ge -w_{b,i}$ (cannot underweight more than the full benchmark weight).

2. Active mean-variance

The standard active-management problem:

$$\max_a \, \alpha^\top a - \tfrac{\lambda}{2} a^\top \Sigma a$$

subject to $\sum a = 0$ and any mandate constraints. Here $\alpha$ is the alpha vector (your forecast of asset excess returns above benchmark expectations).

Or, with explicit TE budget:

$$\max_a \, \alpha^\top a \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \sqrt{a^\top \Sigma a} \le \text{TE}^\star, \, \mathbf{1}^\top a = 0$$

This is a SOCP. Convex, tractable. The Lagrange multiplier on the TE constraint tells you the "price" of risk: how much $\alpha$ would you trade per unit of TE.

3. Beta neutrality

Constraint: $a^\top \beta = 0$ (active portfolio has zero beta to the benchmark). Common in long-short and market-neutral funds.

Implementation: linear constraint on $a$. With cvxpy: cons.append(beta @ a == 0).

Why beta-neutralise? Two reasons:

  1. Performance attribution. The remaining returns are pure alpha, not market exposure.
  2. Risk budgeting. Beta exposure is "cheap" (you can buy it via futures); active alpha is expensive.

4. Sector and country caps

Linear constraints: $\sum_{i \in S} a_i \le c_S$ for each sector or country $S$.

Common in long-only mandates where the portfolio manager wants to bet on stock selection within sectors but not on sector tilt. "Sector neutral" means $c_S = 0$ for all $S$.

5. ESG and other constraints

Modern mandates often include:

  • Carbon intensity cap: $\sum w_i c_i \le \bar c$ where $c_i$ is asset $i$'s carbon footprint.
  • Exclusion lists: $w_i = 0$ for assets in a banned set (tobacco, weapons, etc.).
  • ESG score floor: $\sum w_i e_i \ge \bar e$ where $e_i$ is an ESG score.
  • Turnover cap: $\| w - w_{\text{old}} \|_1 \le \bar t$ per year.

Each is a linear constraint (or quadratic in the case of TE). Add to the optimisation directly. Cost: each binding constraint reduces the achievable IR.

5b. Deep dive: the transfer coefficient (TC) by mandate type

Grinold and Kahn's fundamental law: $\text{IR} = \text{IC} \cdot \sqrt{\text{breadth}} \cdot \text{TC}$. The transfer coefficient TC is what fraction of your alpha you can actually translate into active weights given mandate constraints.

TC ranges by mandate

Mandate Typical TC Why
Long-short hedge fund (no mandate)0.85 to 0.95Can express both long and short alpha. Small slippage from leverage limits.
Active long-only (TE 5%)0.50 to 0.65Cannot short. Underweights capped by benchmark weight.
Long-only with sector neutrality0.40 to 0.55Sector-relative bets only.
Long-only with country and sector neutrality0.30 to 0.45Multiple neutrality constraints stack.

Index fund (TE < 0.5%)0.05 to 0.15By design, almost no active alpha expressed.

Worked example: how mandate eats alpha

Take a manager with IC = 0.06 across 100 stocks rebalancing monthly. Breadth = 100 * 12 = 1200 per year. Unconstrained IR = 0.06 * sqrt(1200) ~ 2.08.

With long-only constraint (TC = 0.5): IR = 1.04.

With long-only + sector neutrality (TC = 0.4): IR = 0.83.

With ESG carbon cap added (TC = 0.35): IR = 0.73.

With turnover cap of 100% per year (TC = 0.30): IR = 0.62.

The same alpha generation produces wildly different outcomes depending on mandate. Boutique hedge funds have higher fees because their unconstrained TC lets them deliver IR > 1. Constrained mutual funds with similar IC can only deliver IR < 0.5 even with the same skill.

The "first constraint costs the most" rule

Long-only is the most expensive single constraint, costing 30 to 50% of TC. After that, additional constraints cost progressively less because they overlap with what long-only already cuts. So the first thing to relax (if you can) is long-only.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Active mean-variance closed form

Without TE budget, the active-MV solution is $a^\star \propto \Sigma^{-1} \alpha - (\mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \alpha / \mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}) \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}$. The second term enforces $\sum a = 0$. With a TE budget, scale the unconstrained solution to hit the budget: $a = c a^{\text{unc}}$ where $c = \text{TE}^\star / \sqrt{a^{\text{unc}\top} \Sigma a^{\text{unc}}}$.

6.2 Grinold-Kahn fundamental law

$\text{IR} = \text{IC} \cdot \sqrt{\text{breadth}} \cdot \text{TC}$ where TC is the transfer coefficient (how cleanly your alpha translates to active weights given constraints). Constraints reduce TC. A long-only fund typically has TC around 0.5; a true long-short has TC near 1.

6.3 The shadow price of TE

Lagrange multiplier on the TE constraint = the marginal alpha per unit of TE. Equals the "TE Sharpe" at the boundary.

6.4 ESG scoring as quadratic

Some ESG frameworks use a "carbon gradient": prefer not just lower carbon but lower carbon variance. Quadratic ESG constraint: $w^\top E w \le \bar e^2$. SOCP again.

6.5 Why long-only constraints reduce TC dramatically

For a 100-stock universe with equal weights, long-only constraint ($a_i \ge -1/100$) caps each underweight at 1%. If your alpha says short by 2% per stock, you can only express 50% of that view. TC drops to ~0.5.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: Active MV with cvxpy

import cvxpy as cp
def active_mv(alpha, Sigma, w_b, te_budget, beta=None, beta_target=None):
    n = len(alpha)
    a = cp.Variable(n)
    obj = cp.Maximize(alpha @ a)
    cons = [cp.sum(a) == 0, cp.quad_form(a, Sigma) <= te_budget ** 2]
    if beta is not None and beta_target is not None:
        cons.append(beta @ (a + w_b) == beta_target)
    cp.Problem(obj, cons).solve()
    return a.value

8. Self test

Question 1, conceptual

Why does the long-only constraint on a fund with 100 stocks reduce the transfer coefficient to ~0.5?

Question 2, coding

Add an ESG score floor to Exercise A: $\sum w_i \cdot e_i \ge 0.5$ where $e_i \in [-1, 1]$. Test on synthetic data.

Question 3, numerical

For TE budget = 3% and IC = 0.05 across 50 names, what is the achievable IR? Use Grinold-Kahn with TC = 0.7 (typical long-only).

Answer: $\text{IR} = 0.05 \cdot \sqrt{50} \cdot 0.7 \approx 0.25$. Modest by hedge-fund standards, decent for long-only.
