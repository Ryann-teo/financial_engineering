0. Reading the controls: what every slider means

A short glossary so you know exactly what each parameter is doing in the math.

$\mu_i$: the asset's mean return (annualised)

The first moment of asset $i$'s return distribution. We slide it from $-5\%$ to $+20\%$ annualised. Means are notoriously hard to estimate from data: even with 30 years of monthly observations, the standard error of an estimated mean is comparable to the mean itself for most assets. This estimation difficulty is the central topic of Lesson 9. For now, think of $\mu_i$ as a forward-looking number that comes from your view (carry, value, macro) rather than from the historical mean.

$\sigma_i$: the asset's volatility (annualised)

The square root of the second central moment, $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[(r_i - \mu_i)^2]}$. Volatility is much easier to estimate than mean. With 1 year of daily data you typically have a good handle on $\sigma_i$. We slide from $5\%$ to $50\%$ annualised. Equity index $\sigma$ sits around $15\%$. Single stock $\sigma$ sits around $25\%$ to $40\%$. Crypto $\sigma$ sits around $60\%$ to $100\%$. Bonds sit around $5\%$ to $10\%$.

$\rho_{ij}$: the correlation between asset $i$ and asset $j$

$\rho_{ij} = \text{Cov}(r_i, r_j) / (\sigma_i \sigma_j)$. By Cauchy-Schwarz, $\rho \in [-1, +1]$. Three regimes matter intuitively:

  • $\rho > 0$: assets move together. Typical equity-equity correlation is 0.4 to 0.8 within a region.
  • $\rho \approx 0$: assets are uncorrelated. Gold-equity often sits here.
  • $\rho < 0$: assets move opposite. Bond-equity in calm markets is negative.

The slider goes from $-0.95$ to $+0.95$. We avoid $\pm 1$ exactly because the covariance matrix becomes singular and cannot be inverted. Singular $\Sigma$ is a feature you will meet again in Lesson 13.

Number of assets (2 vs 3)

Two assets is the smallest interesting case. Closed forms exist, plots are 2D. Three assets is the smallest case where you can see "true" portfolio behaviour: a budget constraint, a covariance matrix with off-diagonal structure, and the shape of the efficient frontier. Anything beyond three is conceptually identical, just higher-dimensional.

Simulated draws (n_sims)

How many points we draw from the joint distribution to plot the cloud and ellipsoid. More draws make the cloud denser and statistical estimates more reliable, but slow rendering. 1500 to 3000 is a good balance.

Joint distribution: Gaussian vs Student t

The Gaussian is the textbook default. The Student t (with df = 4 here) is the more realistic heavy-tailed alternative. Toggle and compare. The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ remains the same in both cases, but the t will produce more extreme joint moves (clusters of points far from the centre).

Weights $w_i$

The portfolio weights. We allow $w_1 \in [-0.5, 1.5]$, with the remaining mass automatically assigned. Negative weight = short. Above 1 = leverage (you borrow to buy more). The constraint $\sum w_i = 1$ is the budget constraint and is the central piece of the Lagrangian we derive in Section 9 below.

1. A portfolio is a linear combination

Suppose you hold weight $w_1$ in asset 1 and $w_2$ in asset 2, with $w_1 + w_2 = 1$. The portfolio return is

$$r_p = w_1 r_1 + w_2 r_2 = w^\top r$$

That is the entire definition. Everything that follows is a consequence of this single linear identity.

For $N$ assets the same formula holds. We use vector notation because it scales: $w$ is the $N$-dimensional weight vector, $r$ is the $N$-dimensional return vector, and $w^\top r$ is the inner product.

2. Expectation is linear

The mean of a sum is the sum of the means. This is one of the few identities in probability that holds without any assumptions about independence or distribution.

$$\mathbb{E}[r_p] = \mathbb{E}[w^\top r] = w^\top \mathbb{E}[r] = w^\top \mu$$

If asset 1 has mean 8% and asset 2 has mean 5%, a 60/40 split has expected return $0.6 \times 8\% + 0.4 \times 5\% = 6.8\%$. No subtleties.

3. Variance is quadratic

This is the heart of portfolio theory. The variance of a sum is not the sum of the variances unless the assets are uncorrelated. The general formula uses the full covariance matrix:

$$\text{Var}(r_p) = \text{Var}(w^\top r) = w^\top \Sigma w$$

To see why, expand for two assets:

$$\text{Var}(w_1 r_1 + w_2 r_2) = w_1^2 \sigma_1^2 + w_2^2 \sigma_2^2 + 2 w_1 w_2 \text{Cov}(r_1, r_2)$$

The cross term $2 w_1 w_2 \text{Cov}(r_1, r_2)$ is what makes diversification possible. If $\text{Cov} < 0$, the cross term is negative, and the portfolio variance is less than the weighted average of the individual variances.

You can write the same expression using $\Sigma$, the covariance matrix:

$$\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1^2 & \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \\ \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2 & \sigma_2^2 \end{bmatrix}$$

The matrix-vector form $w^\top \Sigma w$ generalises to any number of assets without rewriting.

4. The covariance ellipsoid

$\Sigma$ has a beautiful geometric interpretation. The level sets of the multivariate normal density

$$f(r) \propto \exp\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}(r - \mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1} (r - \mu)\right)$$

are ellipsoids centred at $\mu$ with shape determined by $\Sigma$. The principal axes of the ellipsoid point along the eigenvectors of $\Sigma$, and the lengths of the axes are the square roots of the eigenvalues. The largest eigenvalue points along the direction of greatest joint variability.

5. Diversification through correlation

The whole reason portfolio theory exists is the fact that $\rho < 1$ means combining assets reduces total risk below the weighted average of individual risks. This is the only free lunch in finance.

For two assets with equal volatility $\sigma$, an equal weight portfolio has variance

$$\text{Var}(0.5 r_1 + 0.5 r_2) = \frac{\sigma^2(1 + \rho)}{2}$$

If $\rho = 1$, this equals $\sigma^2$ (no benefit). If $\rho = 0$, it equals $\sigma^2 / 2$. If $\rho = -1$, it equals $0$. Perfect anti-correlation eliminates risk completely, but no two real assets are perfectly anti-correlated.

6. The closed form minimum variance portfolio

Now we use calculus. Among all weights with $w_1 + w_2 = 1$, which one minimises portfolio variance? Substitute $w_2 = 1 - w_1$ into the variance formula:

$$V(w_1) = w_1^2 \sigma_1^2 + (1 - w_1)^2 \sigma_2^2 + 2 w_1 (1 - w_1) \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2$$

Take the derivative, set it to zero:

$$\frac{dV}{dw_1} = 2 w_1 \sigma_1^2 - 2 (1 - w_1) \sigma_2^2 + 2 (1 - 2 w_1) \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2 = 0$$

Solve for $w_1$:

$$w_1^\star = \frac{\sigma_2^2 - \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2}{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2 \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2}$$

Two important observations. First, this depends only on $\sigma_1$, $\sigma_2$, $\rho$. The means $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$ do not enter. Means choose your point on the frontier. Covariance chooses the frontier shape. Second, when $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2$, the formula simplifies to $w_1^\star = 1/2$ regardless of $\rho$. Equal volatilities yield equal weights at the minimum variance point.

The Python translation

import numpy as np

def portfolio_var(w, Sigma):
    return float(w @ Sigma @ w)

def min_var_two_asset(s1, s2, rho):
    num = s2**2 - rho * s1 * s2
    den = s1**2 + s2**2 - 2 * rho * s1 * s2
    return num / den

s1, s2, rho = 0.25, 0.10, 0.30
w1_star = min_var_two_asset(s1, s2, rho)
w_star = np.array([w1_star, 1 - w1_star])
Sigma = np.array([[s1**2, rho*s1*s2], [rho*s1*s2, s2**2]])
print(f"w1* = {w1_star:.4f}, var = {portfolio_var(w_star, Sigma):.6f}")

7. Generalising to $N$ assets

Two-asset closed forms get unwieldy. The $N$-asset minimum variance portfolio has a clean matrix expression. Use a Lagrangian for the budget constraint $\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$:

$$\mathcal{L}(w, \lambda) = \tfrac{1}{2} w^\top \Sigma w - \lambda (\mathbf{1}^\top w - 1)$$

Setting $\nabla_w \mathcal{L} = 0$ gives $\Sigma w = \lambda \mathbf{1}$, so $w = \lambda \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}$. The constraint pins down $\lambda$:

$$w_{\min} = \frac{\Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}}$$

This is the workhorse formula. Notice that it requires inverting $\Sigma$. When $\Sigma$ is ill-conditioned (which happens whenever $\rho$ is near $\pm 1$ between any pair of assets, or whenever you have more assets than time periods), $\Sigma^{-1}$ is unstable. That instability is precisely the topic of Lesson 9 (error maximisation) and Lesson 10 (shrinkage).

Why this matters going forward. Every portfolio optimisation problem in this course either uses $\Sigma^{-1}$ directly or sidesteps it (HRP, equal-risk-contribution). When you read a paper, ask: does this method invert $\Sigma$? If yes, what protects it from instability?
In practice (Macrosynergy conceptual parity): The N-asset min-var formula requires inverting $\Sigma$. With $N$ between 50 and 500, $\Sigma^{-1}$ amplifies estimation error by 3 to 4 orders of magnitude (you will see this in Lesson 9). Macrosynergy refuses to invert $\Sigma$ in production. They equal-weight by economic justification: each macro factor receives an equal risk budget regardless of historical covariance. Articles 23 (macro-aware risk parity) and 88 (macro factors for risk parity) are the closest they come to using $\Sigma$ structure, and even there the weights are pinned by economic priors not least-squares. The whole curriculum from Lesson 7 (risk budgeting) through Lesson 15 (HRP) is about ways to allocate without inverting $\Sigma$.

8. Mathematical companion: derivations and proofs

The textbook material that justifies every plot you saw. Read once now, refer back as needed.

8.1 Why expectation is linear

Linearity of expectation says $\mathbb{E}[\sum_i a_i X_i] = \sum_i a_i \mathbb{E}[X_i]$ for any constants $a_i$ and any random variables $X_i$, regardless of independence. The proof is one line: integration is linear. For discrete distributions, $\sum_x \sum_i a_i x_i p(x_i) = \sum_i a_i \sum_x x_i p(x_i)$. For continuous, replace sums with integrals. This is the most underrated identity in probability because it does not require independence.

Applied to portfolios: $\mathbb{E}[w^\top r] = w^\top \mathbb{E}[r] = w^\top \mu$. Asset weights factor cleanly out of the expectation, which is why mean-targeting in mean-variance optimisation is a linear constraint.

8.2 Why variance is quadratic, in full

Variance is the second central moment: $\text{Var}(Y) = \mathbb{E}[(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y])^2]$. For a portfolio:

$$\text{Var}(w^\top r) = \mathbb{E}\left[(w^\top r - w^\top \mu)^2\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[(w^\top (r - \mu))^2\right]$$

The inner term is a scalar, so it equals its own square root squared. Expand:

$$(w^\top (r - \mu))^2 = w^\top (r - \mu)(r - \mu)^\top w$$

Now take expectation. Linearity moves $w^\top$ and $w$ outside:

$$\text{Var}(w^\top r) = w^\top \mathbb{E}[(r - \mu)(r - \mu)^\top] w = w^\top \Sigma w$$

where $\Sigma = \mathbb{E}[(r - \mu)(r - \mu)^\top]$ is the covariance matrix by definition. This is the affine identity in full. Every portfolio optimisation in this course descends from this expression.

8.3 Why $\Sigma$ must be positive semi-definite

For any $w \in \mathbb{R}^N$, $w^\top \Sigma w$ is a variance, so it must be non-negative. By definition, that makes $\Sigma$ positive semi-definite (PSD). Equivalently, all eigenvalues of $\Sigma$ are non-negative.

If $\Sigma$ has a zero eigenvalue, there exists a non-zero $w$ with $w^\top \Sigma w = 0$. That portfolio has zero variance: a perfect hedge. This happens when assets are linearly dependent, which is mathematically rare but practically common in factor models.

If $\Sigma$ has a negative eigenvalue (because of estimation error), the matrix is not a valid covariance and any optimiser using it can return nonsense weights. Lesson 13 teaches you how to clean $\Sigma$ to keep it PSD.

8.4 The covariance ellipsoid, derived

The multivariate normal density is

$$f(r) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{N/2} |\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}(r - \mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1} (r - \mu)\right)$$

Level sets are sets where $f(r) = c$. Take logs and rearrange: a level set of $f$ is a level set of $(r - \mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1} (r - \mu)$, which is a quadratic form. Quadratic forms with PSD matrices have ellipsoidal level sets centred at $\mu$.

The principal axes of the ellipsoid point along the eigenvectors of $\Sigma$. The lengths of the semi-axes are proportional to $\sqrt{\lambda_i}$ where $\lambda_i$ are the eigenvalues of $\Sigma$. The largest eigenvalue points along the direction of greatest joint variability.

The 1-sigma ellipse drawn in the demo above is the level set at $\mu + \sqrt{\lambda_i} v_i$. About 39% of a 2D normal sample falls inside it; the rest is the rim and beyond.

8.5 The condition number and why it matters

$\kappa(\Sigma) = \lambda_{\max}(\Sigma) / \lambda_{\min}(\Sigma)$ measures how stretched the ellipse is. Round ellipse: $\kappa \approx 1$. Pancake: $\kappa \to \infty$. Inversion of $\Sigma$ amplifies any error by roughly $\kappa$. So if $\kappa = 1000$ and your estimated $\Sigma$ has 1% measurement error, the implied $\Sigma^{-1}$ has up to 1000% error.

This is the formal statement of the "error maximisation" problem with mean-variance optimisation, which we tackle head-on in Lesson 9. Real-world covariance matrices on 50 to 500 assets routinely have $\kappa$ in the thousands, which is why naive Markowitz blows up out of sample.

8.6 The Lagrangian derivation of the minimum variance portfolio

Goal: minimise $\tfrac{1}{2} w^\top \Sigma w$ subject to $\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$. The Lagrangian is

$$\mathcal{L}(w, \lambda) = \tfrac{1}{2} w^\top \Sigma w - \lambda(\mathbf{1}^\top w - 1)$$

Stationarity in $w$:

$$\nabla_w \mathcal{L} = \Sigma w - \lambda \mathbf{1} = 0 \implies w = \lambda \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}$$

Apply the budget constraint:

$$\mathbf{1}^\top w = \lambda \mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1} = 1 \implies \lambda = \frac{1}{\mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}}$$

Therefore

$$w_{\min} = \frac{\Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}}$$

The numerator is a vector, the denominator is a scalar. The denominator just normalises the weights to sum to 1.

8.7 Why $\mu$ does not enter the minimum variance formula

This is the geometric heart of the matter. The objective $w^\top \Sigma w$ is a function of $w$ only. The means $\mu$ do not appear in it. The constraint $\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$ also does not involve $\mu$. So the optimal $w$ cannot depend on $\mu$.

Geometrically, the level sets of $w^\top \Sigma w$ in weight space are ellipsoids centred at the origin. The constraint $\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$ is a hyperplane. The minimum variance point is where the smallest ellipsoid touches the hyperplane. This intersection depends on $\Sigma$ and $\mathbf{1}$, not on $\mu$. Means choose your point on the frontier. Covariance chooses the frontier shape.

8.8 The two-fund separation theorem (preview of Lesson 5)

Tobin (1958) showed: the entire Markowitz efficient frontier can be generated by combining just two distinct portfolios. Any efficient portfolio is a linear combination of the minimum variance portfolio and the tangency portfolio (the one that maximises Sharpe). This is why most investment products bundle into "core" (the tangency or risk-asset bucket) and "cash" (effectively the minimum-variance asset).

We derive this carefully in Lesson 5 of Module B.

8.9 Sanity-check: the diversification formula at $\rho = 0$

Two assets with $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = \sigma$ and equal weights: $\text{Var} = \sigma^2 (1 + \rho) / 2$. At $\rho = 0$, this is $\sigma^2 / 2$. Generalise to $N$ uncorrelated assets with equal weights and equal volatility:

$$\text{Var}(\bar{r}) = \text{Var}\left(\tfrac{1}{N}\sum_i r_i\right) = \frac{\sigma^2}{N}$$

Volatility falls as $\sigma / \sqrt{N}$. This is the diversification rule of $\sqrt{N}$. The catch: real assets are correlated. Adding a 51st correlated equity to a 50-stock portfolio reduces vol by less than $\sqrt{51}/\sqrt{50}$ would predict.

Exercise A: Verify the affine identity by Monte Carlo

Generate 100,000 samples from a multivariate normal, compute the empirical variance of samples @ w, compare to w @ Sigma @ w. They should match to about 0.5%.

def verify_affine(w, mu, Sigma, n=100_000, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    samples = rng.multivariate_normal(mu, Sigma, size=n)
    empirical = samples @ w
    return dict(empirical_var=empirical.var(ddof=1), theoretical_var=w @ Sigma @ w)

Exercise B: 2x2 inverse by hand

For a 2x2 covariance matrix, write out $\Sigma^{-1}$ using the formula $\Sigma^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det \Sigma} \begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix}$ for $\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$. Verify numerically that $\Sigma \Sigma^{-1} = I$.

def inverse_2x2(Sigma):
    a, b = Sigma[0, 0], Sigma[0, 1]
    c, d = Sigma[1, 0], Sigma[1, 1]
    det = a * d - b * c
    return np.array([[d, -b], [-c, a]]) / det

Exercise C: Maximum diversification portfolio

The Choueifaty-Coignard MDP maximises the diversification ratio. Solve numerically.

from scipy.optimize import minimize

def max_diversification_portfolio(Sigma):
    n = Sigma.shape[0]
    sigmas = np.sqrt(np.diag(Sigma))
    def neg_dr(w):
        if w.sum() == 0: return 0
        return -(w @ sigmas) / np.sqrt(w @ Sigma @ w)
    constraints = [{'type': 'eq', 'fun': lambda w: w.sum() - 1}]
    bounds = [(0, 1)] * n
    result = minimize(neg_dr, np.ones(n) / n, constraints=constraints, bounds=bounds, method='SLSQP')
    return result.x

Exercise D: ERC by cyclical coordinate descent

Equal Risk Contribution: each asset contributes equally to portfolio variance. Solve via coordinate descent.

def erc_portfolio(Sigma, max_iter=1000, tol=1e-9):
    n = Sigma.shape[0]
    w = np.ones(n) / n
    for _ in range(max_iter):
        w_old = w.copy()
        for i in range(n):
            num = (Sigma @ w)[i] - Sigma[i, i] * w[i]
            disc = num ** 2 + 4 * Sigma[i, i] * (1 / n) * (w @ Sigma @ w)
            w[i] = (-num + np.sqrt(disc)) / (2 * Sigma[i, i])
        w = w / w.sum()
        if np.linalg.norm(w - w_old) < tol:
            break
    return w

Exercise E: Simulate the Markowitz "error maximisation" effect

Take Sigma_true and mu_true. Add Gaussian noise to mu_true with standard deviation 0.005. Solve the tangency portfolio. Repeat 200 times. Plot the distribution of weights. Compare to the same exercise on min-var (which is more stable).

def error_maximisation(mu, Sigma, sigma_noise=0.005, n_trials=200, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    weights = []
    for _ in range(n_trials):
        mu_pert = mu + sigma_noise * rng.standard_normal(len(mu))
        excess = mu_pert - 0.02
        w = np.linalg.solve(Sigma, excess)
        w = w / w.sum()
        weights.append(w)
    return np.array(weights)

10. Self test

Question 1, conceptual

Why does $w^\star_{\min}$ for two assets depend only on volatilities and correlation, not on means? Hint: write out $\frac{dV}{dw_1}$. The means are constants when you take the variance.

Question 2, geometric

You hold a portfolio with $w = [0.5, 0.5]$, $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = 20\%$, $\rho = 0$. The portfolio vol is $\sqrt{0.5^2 \cdot 0.04 + 0.5^2 \cdot 0.04} = \sqrt{0.02} \approx 14.1\%$. Now $\rho$ drops to $-1$. What is the new portfolio vol?

Answer: 0%. With perfect anti-correlation, equal weight on equal-vol assets eliminates all variance. Verify in the demo above by setting both vols equal and $\rho = -0.95$.

Question 3, coding

Implement the $N$-asset minimum variance formula. Test it on a 3-asset case with $\sigma = [0.20, 0.15, 0.10]$ and pairwise correlations all 0.3. The optimal weights should sum to 1 and the portfolio vol should be smaller than any individual asset vol.

def min_var_n_asset(Sigma):
    inv = np.linalg.inv(Sigma)
    ones = np.ones(Sigma.shape[0])
    w = inv @ ones
    return w / w.sum()
