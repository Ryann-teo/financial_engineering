0. Reading the controls

Mean perturbation $\sigma_\mu$

Standard deviation of the noise we add to each asset's expected return $\mu_i$. Real $\mu$ estimates have standard errors of around 1 to 3 percent annualised even with 30 years of monthly data. Slide $\sigma_\mu$ to see what this estimation noise does to the optimal weights.

Number of bootstrap trials

How many perturbed $\mu$ vectors we draw, each producing a different "optimal" portfolio. The spread of these portfolios tells you the estimation risk.

Method toggle

Tangency (max Sharpe) vs minimum variance vs equal weight. Tangency uses both $\mu$ and $\Sigma$. Min-var uses only $\Sigma$. Equal weight uses neither. Watch which method's weights are stable.

Universe size $N$

Number of assets. As $N$ approaches the sample length $T$, the sample covariance becomes singular and $\Sigma^{-1}$ explodes.

1. The error maximisation problem

Mean-variance optimisation looks for the portfolio whose weighted average return is highest relative to its variance. When the input $\mu$ has estimation error, the optimiser does something perverse: it preferentially loads up on assets whose $\mu$ has been overestimated by chance. The errors do not cancel out across assets; they compound.

This phenomenon was named by Michaud (1989). The math:

$$w^\star \propto \Sigma^{-1} (\mu - r_f \mathbf{1})$$

If your estimate $\hat \mu$ has error $\epsilon$, then $\hat w^\star = w^\star + \Sigma^{-1} \epsilon$. The error in weights is $\Sigma^{-1}$ times the error in means. When $\Sigma^{-1}$ is large in magnitude (which it is when $\Sigma$ has small eigenvalues), the weight error explodes.

2. Sensitivity of tangency vs min-var

Tangency is the worst offender because it requires accurate $\mu$. Minimum variance is more robust because it does not depend on $\mu$ at all (only on $\Sigma$). The diagnostic: bootstrap the input data, re-solve, look at the spread of optimal weights.

3. Condition number and the geometry of $\Sigma^{-1}$

The condition number of a matrix is the ratio of its largest eigenvalue to its smallest:

$$\kappa(\Sigma) = \frac{\lambda_{\max}(\Sigma)}{\lambda_{\min}(\Sigma)}$$

$\kappa = 1$ means rotational symmetry (round ellipse). $\kappa$ large means a pancake. Inverting $\Sigma$ amplifies any error in $\Sigma$ or $\mu$ by roughly $\kappa$. Real-world $\kappa$ on 50 to 500 asset universes routinely exceeds 1000.

What blows up $\kappa$? Two things in the data, and one in the universe choice:

  1. Highly correlated assets. If two assets have $\rho = 0.99$, the ellipse pancakes along their axis.
  2. Low-vol assets mixed with high-vol. Bonds with $\sigma = 5\%$ and crypto with $\sigma = 80\%$ produce eigenvalue gaps of 256x just from the diagonal.
  3. $T \approx N$. When sample length $T$ is close to the number of assets $N$, the smallest eigenvalues approach zero by Marcenko-Pastur. Condition number diverges.

4. The high-dimensional regime

Random matrix theory tells us that for $T$ observations on $N$ assets with true covariance $I$, the sample covariance has eigenvalues spread over the Marcenko-Pastur interval

$$\lambda_\pm = (1 \pm \sqrt{N/T})^2$$

The smallest eigenvalue is $(1 - \sqrt{N/T})^2$. When $N = T$, it hits zero exactly. The condition number is

$$\kappa = \left(\frac{1 + \sqrt{N/T}}{1 - \sqrt{N/T}}\right)^2$$

For $N/T = 0.5$, $\kappa \approx 33$. For $N/T = 0.9$, $\kappa \approx 360$. For $N/T = 0.99$, $\kappa \approx 40000$. The cliff is real and steep.

5. DeMiguel, Garlappi, Uppal (2009)

The paper that put a number on the failure. DGU compared 14 mean-variance strategies on seven datasets. Their finding: none of the optimised methods beat 1/N (equal weight) consistently out of sample, after costs. The required sample length to make optimisation pay over equal weight was roughly 3000 months for a 25-asset universe. Nobody has 3000 months.

The conclusions of DGU were controversial but the empirical pattern is robust: a method that uses $\Sigma^{-1}$ on noisy data performs worse than a method that does not. The remainder of Module C is about how to fix this.

In practice (Macrosynergy, conceptual parity): Macrosynergy does not run Markowitz in production. Their answer to error maximisation is conceptual parity: equal-weight macro signals within a theme, equal-weight themes across an asset, equal-weight assets across a strategy. Articles 3 (cross-asset relative value), 28 (signal optimisation), and 50 (signal evaluation) all explicitly avoid optimising weights. The Sharpe loss vs an "ideal" optimised weighting is small. The robustness gain is large. This is the empirical philosophy that DGU formalised.

5b. Deep dive: a worked numerical example

Concrete numbers always beat abstract math. Let's work through error maximisation on a 2-asset problem you can verify by hand.

Setup

Two assets. True parameters:

  • $\mu_1 = 8\%$, $\mu_2 = 4\%$ annualised.
  • $\sigma_1 = 16\%$, $\sigma_2 = 6\%$.
  • $\rho = 0.20$.

True covariance: $\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} 0.0256 & 0.00192 \\ 0.00192 & 0.0036 \end{bmatrix}$.

The true tangency weights (using $r_f = 0$): $w_T \propto \Sigma^{-1} \mu = (3.5, 9.2)$ before normalisation. After scaling to sum to 1: $w_T \approx (0.28, 0.72)$. Tangency Sharpe: 0.85.

Add 200 bp of noise to $\mu$

Suppose your estimate is $\hat\mu_1 = 8\% + 2\% = 10\%$ (overestimated by 200 bp). Re-solve the tangency:

$\hat w_T \propto \Sigma^{-1} (0.10, 0.04)^\top = (4.3, 8.8)$. After normalisation: $\hat w_T \approx (0.33, 0.67)$.

So a 200 bp upward bias on asset 1's mean shifted its weight from 28% to 33%. The weight is much more sensitive to noise than the input itself. Asset 1's weight increased by 5 percentage points (18% relative) for a 200 bp absolute error.

Now overestimate by 500 bp

$\hat w_T \approx (0.42, 0.58)$. Now we are 50% overweight asset 1 vs the true optimum. The "best" portfolio under this noisy estimate is a quite different allocation.

The condition number explains it

$\Sigma$ has eigenvalues 0.026 and 0.0035. Condition number $\kappa = 7.4$. The weight error is approximately $\kappa$ times the relative input error. Across a large universe with $\kappa$ in the hundreds or thousands, the amplification is brutal.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 The derivative of optimal weights with respect to $\mu$

For unconstrained mean-variance with budget constraint $\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$:

$$\frac{\partial w^\star}{\partial \mu} = \frac{1}{\lambda} \left(\Sigma^{-1} - \frac{\Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1}}{\mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}}\right)$$

The norm of this matrix is dominated by $\lambda_{\max}(\Sigma^{-1}) = 1 / \lambda_{\min}(\Sigma)$. Small eigenvalues of $\Sigma$ create large gradients.

6.2 Marcenko-Pastur for sample covariance

For $T \times N$ random Gaussian data with true covariance $I$, the empirical eigenvalue density of the sample covariance converges to the Marcenko-Pastur distribution:

$$f(\lambda) = \frac{1}{2\pi \sigma^2} \frac{\sqrt{(\lambda_+ - \lambda)(\lambda - \lambda_-)}}{\lambda} \cdot \mathbb{1}_{[\lambda_-, \lambda_+]}$$

with $\lambda_\pm = \sigma^2 (1 \pm \sqrt{N/T})^2$. This tells you exactly how spread out your sample eigenvalues should be when there is no real covariance structure. The cleaning step in Lesson 13 cuts eigenvalues that fall inside this interval (treating them as noise).

6.3 Why min-var is more stable than tangency

The min-var weight $w_{\min} = \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1} / (\mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1})$ does not depend on $\mu$ at all. The tangency weight $w_T \propto \Sigma^{-1}(\mu - r_f \mathbf{1})$ has the additional sensitivity to $\mu$. So tangency variance equals min-var variance plus a term scaled by $\text{Var}(\mu)$. Empirically, min-var weights have 3 to 5 times smaller bootstrap standard error than tangency weights.

6.4 The "James-Stein" cure preview

James-Stein (1961) showed that for the multivariate mean estimation problem with $N \ge 3$, the sample mean is dominated by a shrinkage estimator. Shrink your mean estimate toward the grand mean (or any fixed target) and you reduce mean squared error in expectation. The same principle works for $\Sigma$ (Lesson 10) and is the formal basis for Bayesian portfolio choice.

6.5 Why 1/N is hard to beat

Equal weight has zero estimation error in $\mu$ (none used), and uses no $\Sigma$ inverse (no amplification). Its only "error" is using uniform weights when the true optimal is non-uniform. DGU show that for typical estimation noise, the bias from uniform weights is smaller than the variance from optimisation. Equal weight's "model" is $\mu = c \mathbf{1}$ for some $c$, $\Sigma = \sigma^2 I$. This null model is wrong, but it is wrong in a robust way.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: Bootstrap weight distribution

def bootstrap_weights(mu, Sigma, sigma_mu=0.005, n_trials=200, method='tangency', seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    weights = []
    for _ in range(n_trials):
        mu_pert = mu + sigma_mu * rng.standard_normal(len(mu))
        if method == 'tangency':
            w = np.linalg.solve(Sigma, mu_pert - 0.02)
            w = w / w.sum()
        elif method == 'minvar':
            w = np.linalg.solve(Sigma, np.ones(len(mu)))
            w = w / w.sum()
        else:
            w = np.ones(len(mu)) / len(mu)
        weights.append(w)
    return np.array(weights)

Exercise B: Condition number sweep

For a 20-asset universe, sample $T$ from 30 to 1000. Plot $\kappa(\hat \Sigma)$ vs $T$. Compare to the Marcenko-Pastur prediction.

Exercise C: DeMiguel-style horse race

On real or synthetic data, compute out-of-sample Sharpe for: tangency, min-var, equal weight. Use rolling 60-month windows. Tabulate Sharpe and turnover. Equal weight should be competitive.

Exercise D: Marcenko-Pastur visualisation

Generate 1000 trials of $N=50$, $T=100$ Gaussian data with true covariance $I$. Plot the histogram of eigenvalues. Overlay the analytical MP density. They should match.

Exercise E: The role of correlations

Construct two universes, both with $N=10$. Universe A has block-diagonal correlation (3 blocks of 0.9 within, 0.0 between). Universe B has uniform 0.3 correlation. Compute $\kappa$ and bootstrap weight std for tangency. A should be far worse than B.
