0. Reading the controls

Bid-ask cost (bp per side)

Half the bid-ask spread. For US large-cap equities: 1 to 2 bp. For mid-cap: 5 to 10 bp. For EM equities: 10 to 30 bp. For OTC corporate bonds: 20 to 100 bp.

Impact coefficient $\eta$

The square-root impact constant. Almgren et al. (2005) measured $\eta \approx 0.142$ across NYSE stocks. Higher for less liquid markets.

Rebalance frequency

Daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly. The single biggest lever for cost control.

1. Cost taxonomy

  • Bid-ask spread. Half the spread paid per side of any trade. For liquid ETFs, 0.5 to 2 bp.
  • Commissions. Fixed fee per trade. Negligible for institutional.
  • Market impact. The price moves against you as you trade. Roughly $\sqrt{\text{trade size} / \text{ADV}}$.
  • Financing. Borrowing cost above risk-free rate for leveraged positions.
  • Borrow. Cost to borrow shares to short-sell. Hard-to-borrow names can cost 5 to 50% per year.
  • Opportunity cost. Trade slippage from delays in execution.

2. Linear cost model

The simplest cost-aware mean-variance:

$$\max_w \, w^\top \mu - \tfrac{\lambda}{2} w^\top \Sigma w - \kappa \| w - w_{\text{old}} \|_1$$

$\kappa$ is the proportional cost (bp / 100 / 2). The $\ell_1$ penalty is the total trade volume. This is a convex QP, easy to solve.

3. Square-root market impact

Almgren (2005), Frazzini-Israel-Moskowitz (2018): market impact scales as the square root of trade size. For a trade of size $|\Delta w_i|$ as a fraction of ADV, the impact cost is

$$\text{cost}_i = \eta \sigma_i \sqrt{|\Delta w_i|}$$

This is a concave (in trade size) cost. Solving cost-aware MV with square-root impact is a SOCP. Slightly harder than linear but still tractable in cvxpy.

4. The no-trade region

Under proportional costs, the optimal policy has a "no-trade region": a set of portfolio configurations where trading is not worth it. Inside this region, do nothing. Outside, trade to the boundary.

For a single-asset Mertonian problem with proportional costs (Davis-Norman 1990), the no-trade region is a band around the cost-free optimum. The width of the band scales as $\sqrt[3]{\kappa}$.

5. Garleanu-Pedersen optimal trading

Garleanu and Pedersen (2013) derive the optimal multi-period trading rule under quadratic costs and predictable returns:

$$w_t^\star = w_{t-1} + (1 - \rho)(w_{\text{aim},t} - w_{t-1})$$

where $w_{\text{aim},t}$ is the cost-free target for period $t$ and $\rho$ is a smoothing factor that depends on the cost-to-risk ratio. The intuition: partial adjustment. Do not trade fully to the target each period; instead trade a fraction of the way. The fraction depends on how persistent the alpha is and how costly the trade.

6. Rebalancing frequency choice

Two extremes:

  • Daily. Captures fast signal decay but high turnover and cost. Good for high-IC, fast-decay signals.
  • Quarterly. Low turnover, low cost. Good for slow signals (value, fundamental).

Heuristic: pick the frequency where $\text{IC} \cdot \sqrt{\text{frequency}} \approx \text{cost} \cdot \text{turnover per rebalance} / \text{vol}$. Equating expected alpha per rebalance with expected cost per rebalance.

In practice (Macrosynergy art 91, 366): Article 91 ("Transaction costs and portfolio strategies") quantifies cost drag across MS strategies. Article 366 covers bid-offer in OTC markets specifically. Macrosynergy's production default: monthly rebalancing for slow macro signals, weekly for trend, daily only for very high-frequency signals (rare). They use the L1 penalty rather than full square-root impact because the cvxpy speed is critical for hyperparameter tuning across many strategies.

7. Mathematical companion

7.1 The L1 penalty as turnover proxy

$\| w - w_{\text{old}} \|_1 = \sum_i |\Delta w_i|$ is the total trade volume in $\ell_1$ norm. Multiplying by $\kappa$ (cost per unit traded) gives the dollar cost. Convex, piecewise linear; cvxpy handles via the simplex algorithm.

7.2 Square-root impact in convex form

$\sqrt{|\Delta w_i|}$ is concave, so $-\sqrt{|\Delta w_i|}$ is convex. The full impact cost is a concave function of trade size, but for optimisation we minimise $-\sqrt{|\Delta w_i|}$ which is convex. cvxpy: cp.norm(cp.abs(w - w_old), 0.5) or use the conic approximation.

7.3 The Davis-Norman no-trade band

For a single-asset Mertonian with proportional costs, the no-trade band has width

$$\Delta w \approx \left(\frac{6 \kappa \sigma^2}{\gamma}\right)^{1/3}$$

where $\kappa$ is the cost rate and $\gamma$ is risk aversion. The cube root means small costs produce small bands; large costs produce large bands.

7.4 Garleanu-Pedersen partial adjustment

Under quadratic costs $C(\Delta w) = \tfrac{1}{2} \Delta w^\top \Lambda \Delta w$, the optimal policy is

$$w_t = (I - M) w_{t-1} + M \bar w_t$$

where $M$ is a matrix that depends on $\Lambda$, $\Sigma$, and the persistence of $\bar w_t$. For diagonal $\Lambda$, $M$ is also diagonal with each entry between 0 and 1 (the partial adjustment coefficient).

7.5 The square-root law's empirical foundation

Almgren et al. (2005) regress realised execution price impact on trade size across thousands of NYSE trades. The fit is approximately

$$\Delta P / P \approx \eta \sigma \sqrt{Q / \text{ADV}}$$

with $\eta \approx 0.142$ for US large caps. This empirical relationship holds across asset classes with different $\eta$.

8. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: Cost-aware MV with cvxpy

import cvxpy as cp
def cost_aware_mv(mu, Sigma, lam, w_old, kappa):
    n = len(mu)
    w = cp.Variable(n)
    obj = cp.Maximize(mu @ w - 0.5 * lam * cp.quad_form(w, Sigma) - kappa * cp.norm(w - w_old, 1))
    cp.Problem(obj, [cp.sum(w) == 1]).solve()
    return w.value
