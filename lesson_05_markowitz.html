0. Reading the controls

Risk aversion $\lambda$

The dial that picks your point on the efficient frontier. The Markowitz objective is $w^\top \mu - (\lambda / 2) w^\top \Sigma w$. As $\lambda \to 0$ the optimum chases the highest mean (highest risk). As $\lambda \to \infty$ the optimum collapses to the minimum variance portfolio. Typical values for institutional investors sit between 1 and 10. Hedge funds run lower (0.5 to 2). Pension funds run higher (5 to 20).

Risk-free rate $r_f$

The return on cash. Used to construct the tangency portfolio (which maximises Sharpe). Typical values: 0% in the post-2009 era, 4 to 5% in 2024 to 2026, 0 to 6% as a long-run range.

Asset means and volatilities

Same as Lesson 2. Five-asset universe with sliders for individual $\mu_i$ and $\sigma_i$. Correlations are fixed at a realistic structure (equity-equity 0.5, bond-equity -0.2, gold-everything 0.0 to 0.1).

1. Four ways to write the same problem

Markowitz (1952) opened the modern theory by writing one problem. Subsequent decades wrote it three more times, each version equivalent to the others under the right Lagrangian.

Form A: max utility. $\max_w w^\top \mu - (\lambda/2) w^\top \Sigma w$ subject to $\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$. The objective is mean-variance utility (quadratic utility, equivalently). $\lambda$ is a free parameter that picks where on the frontier you sit.

Form B: max return at fixed risk. $\max_w w^\top \mu$ subject to $w^\top \Sigma w \le \sigma^2_{\text{target}}$ and $\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$. Fix risk, push return.

Form C: min risk at fixed return. $\min_w w^\top \Sigma w$ subject to $w^\top \mu \ge \mu_{\text{target}}$ and $\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$. Fix return, push risk down.

Form D: max Sharpe. $\max_w (w^\top \mu - r_f) / \sqrt{w^\top \Sigma w}$ subject to $\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$. The tangency portfolio. Maximises return per unit of volatility.

All four forms trace the same efficient frontier. They differ only in how they parameterise the trade-off. Form A is computationally easiest because the Lagrangian has a linear gradient. Forms B and C have quadratic constraints. Form D has a non-convex objective until you reformulate it (one of the rare cases in this course where reformulation matters).

2. The closed-form solutions

Without inequality constraints, Form A has a clean closed form. Take the Lagrangian:

$$\mathcal{L}(w, \kappa) = w^\top \mu - (\lambda/2) w^\top \Sigma w - \kappa (\mathbf{1}^\top w - 1)$$

Set $\nabla_w \mathcal{L} = 0$:

$$\mu - \lambda \Sigma w - \kappa \mathbf{1} = 0 \implies w = \frac{1}{\lambda} \Sigma^{-1} (\mu - \kappa \mathbf{1})$$

Apply the budget constraint to solve for $\kappa$. The result, with two scalar quantities $A = \mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}$ and $B = \mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mu$:

$$w_\lambda = \frac{1}{A} \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1} + \frac{1}{\lambda}\left(\Sigma^{-1} \mu - \frac{B}{A} \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}\right)$$

Reads: the optimal portfolio is the minimum variance portfolio plus a "speculative" tilt scaled by $1/\lambda$. When $\lambda \to \infty$, the tilt vanishes and you sit at min-var. When $\lambda \to 0$, the tilt explodes (which is why we need risk aversion to make the problem well-posed).

The minimum variance portfolio is $w_{\min} = \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1} / A$ (Lesson 2). The tangency portfolio is

$$w_T = \frac{\Sigma^{-1}(\mu - r_f \mathbf{1})}{\mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1}(\mu - r_f \mathbf{1})}$$

Both are linear functions of $\Sigma^{-1}$ applied to a vector.

3. The efficient frontier

4. Tangency portfolio and the capital market line

Add a risk-free asset. Now we have one more "asset" with $\mu = r_f$ and $\sigma = 0$. Any portfolio is a mix of cash and the tangency portfolio. The locus of all such mixes is a straight line in $(\sigma, \mu)$ space, called the Capital Market Line:

$$\mu_p = r_f + \frac{\mu_T - r_f}{\sigma_T} \sigma_p$$

The slope is the tangency Sharpe. The CML lies tangent to the efficient frontier at $w_T$. Any point on the CML strictly dominates any point on the (risky-only) efficient frontier in mean-variance space.

The economic conclusion: every investor holds the same risky portfolio (the tangency), scaled up or down by their preference for risk. This is the celebrated two-fund separation.

5. The two-fund separation theorem

Two distinct efficient portfolios are enough to span the entire frontier. Tobin (1958) proved this. Concretely, every efficient portfolio is a linear combination of $w_{\min}$ and $w_T$:

$$w_\lambda = (1 - \alpha_\lambda) w_{\min} + \alpha_\lambda w_T$$

for some scalar $\alpha_\lambda$ depending on $\lambda$. The investment universe collapses to two funds. This is why most asset managers structure products as "core" (the tangency or risk-asset bucket) and "cash" (or short-duration bonds, the closest practical thing to the risk-free asset).

In practice (Macrosynergy): MS does not implement Markowitz directly because of the estimation error problem (Lessons 9 to 13). They get the spirit of two-fund separation differently: every strategy is a vol-targeted bet, and the portfolio is a scaled mix of the strategy plus cash. The "tangency" role is played by the diversified strategy book (e.g. the cross-asset relative value trade in art 3), not by an optimised tangency portfolio. The economic logic of two-fund separation survives the death of $\Sigma^{-1}$.

5b. Deep dive: risk aversion $\lambda$ in detail

The risk aversion parameter $\lambda$ is the central dial of the Markowitz problem. It deserves its own discussion because most people pick it badly.

What $\lambda$ actually is

$\lambda$ is the Lagrange multiplier on a hidden variance constraint. The equivalence:

$$\max_w \, w^\top \mu - \tfrac{\lambda}{2} w^\top \Sigma w \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \max_w \, w^\top \mu \text{ s.t. } w^\top \Sigma w \le \bar v$$

For a given $\bar v$, there is a unique $\lambda$ that produces the same optimum. Sliding $\lambda$ traces the entire frontier; each $\lambda$ corresponds to a different tolerance for risk.

Calibrating $\lambda$ from data

Three ways:

  1. Implied from market portfolio. $\lambda = (\mu_m - r_f) / \sigma_m^2$. For SPY: $(0.06) / (0.16)^2 \approx 2.3$. The "Black-Litterman default".
  2. From CRRA utility. $\lambda = \gamma / (1 + \gamma w^\top \mu)$ approximately, where $\gamma$ is the CRRA coefficient. For $\gamma = 4$ and small $w^\top \mu$: $\lambda \approx 4$.
  3. From target volatility. $\lambda = \mu^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mu / \sigma_{\text{target}}^2$ approximately. Target 10% vol from a tangency-Sharpe-1.0 universe gives $\lambda \approx 100$. Yes, the units are odd.

Reasonable $\lambda$ ranges by investor type

Investor type Typical $\lambda$ Why
Hedge fund (aggressive)0.5 to 2Target vol 15 to 25%, leverage allowed.
Multi-strategy2 to 5Target vol 8 to 12%, balanced.
Pension fund5 to 15Long-horizon, regulated, conservative.
Insurance10 to 30Liability-driven, capital-constrained.

The $\lambda$ trap: arbitrarily small or large is wrong

$\lambda \to 0$ means "infinite risk appetite": the optimiser concentrates 100% in the highest-mean asset (or shorts the lowest). $\lambda \to \infty$ collapses to minimum variance, ignoring expected returns entirely. Neither extreme is a real preference. Pick from the table or calibrate to a vol target.

The "wandering $\lambda$" production trick

In practice, many shops decouple $\lambda$ from preferences and use it as a turnover dial instead. Higher $\lambda$ produces lower-tracking-error portfolios; lower $\lambda$ produces more aggressive ones. The expected-return scaling is then absorbed by the magnitude of $\mu$.

6. Mathematical companion

6.1 Why the frontier is a hyperbola

The minimum variance for a given target return $\mu_p$ is the value of the constrained optimisation

$$\sigma_p^2(\mu_p) = \min_w w^\top \Sigma w \text{ s.t. } \mathbf{1}^\top w = 1, w^\top \mu = \mu_p$$

The Lagrangian gives a closed form. Let $A = \mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}$, $B = \mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mu$, $C = \mu^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mu$. Then

$$\sigma_p^2(\mu_p) = \frac{A \mu_p^2 - 2 B \mu_p + C}{AC - B^2}$$

This is a quadratic in $\mu_p$ in the denominator-free numerator, which means the relation between $\sigma_p$ and $\mu_p$ is a hyperbola in $(\sigma, \mu)$ space (with vertex at the minimum variance portfolio).

6.2 The Sharpe of the tangency portfolio

The maximised Sharpe ratio is

$$\text{SR}_T = \sqrt{(\mu - r_f \mathbf{1})^\top \Sigma^{-1} (\mu - r_f \mathbf{1})}$$

This is the upper bound on Sharpe for any portfolio in this universe. No combination of these assets achieves a higher Sharpe than the tangency portfolio.

6.3 Why the tangency is the same for everyone (under MV)

The tangency formula is independent of $\lambda$. Two investors with different risk aversions both buy $w_T$ in proportion to their risk appetite. They differ only in how much they leverage or de-leverage that single position. This is the mathematical core of the CAPM (Sharpe 1964): if everyone holds the tangency, the tangency must be the market portfolio in equilibrium.

6.4 Quadratic utility and its discontents

Mean-variance utility $u(W) = \mathbb{E}[W] - (\lambda/2) \text{Var}(W)$ corresponds to quadratic utility $u(W) = W - (a/2) W^2$ for an appropriate $a$. Quadratic utility has a fatal flaw: it is non-monotone. For large $W$, marginal utility becomes negative (you would prefer less wealth). Empirically and theoretically, quadratic utility is wrong. But mean-variance optimisation can still be justified under elliptical return distributions (where higher moments are functions of mean and variance), regardless of utility form. Most of the time we use Markowitz as a reasonable approximation, not a derived consequence of quadratic utility.

6.5 Connection to ordinary least squares

The tangency formula $w_T \propto \Sigma^{-1}(\mu - r_f \mathbf{1})$ has the same shape as the OLS coefficient $\hat\beta = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y$. This is not an accident: in the mean-variance regression of returns on a portfolio's return, the optimal "weights" are exactly the regression coefficients of one set of returns on another. Mean-variance optimisation and linear regression share a common geometry.

7. Coding exercises (extras)

Exercise A: Implement Markowitz with cvxpy

import cvxpy as cp
import numpy as np

def markowitz(mu, Sigma, lam):
    n = len(mu)
    w = cp.Variable(n)
    obj = cp.Maximize(mu @ w - 0.5 * lam * cp.quad_form(w, Sigma))
    cp.Problem(obj, [cp.sum(w) == 1]).solve()
    return w.value

Exercise B: Trace the efficient frontier numerically

Sweep $\lambda$ over a log grid from 0.01 to 100. For each, solve Markowitz. Plot the frontier.

Exercise C: Verify the two-fund theorem

Pick three values of $\lambda$. Solve Markowitz at each. Check that all three optimal weights are linear combinations of $w_{\min}$ and $w_T$. Numerically: stack the three weight vectors as rows. The matrix should have rank 2.

Exercise D: Tangency Sharpe upper bound

Compute $w_T$ analytically and the maximum achievable Sharpe. Compare with a random sample of 1000 random feasible portfolios. None should beat the tangency Sharpe.

Exercise E: Closed-form vs numerical

For an unconstrained problem, the closed form is exact. Solve the same problem with cvxpy and verify the weights match to 6 decimals. Then add a long-only constraint and observe the divergence.
